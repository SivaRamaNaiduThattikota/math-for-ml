Backprop (Session 10) hands you the gradient — the direction of steepest ascent of the loss. Gradient descent is what you do with it: take a small step the opposite way, recompute, repeat. That five-word loop — step downhill, over and over — is how essentially every model on earth is trained. Everything sophisticated in optimization (momentum, Adam, learning-rate schedules) is a refinement of this one idea.

This session closes the calculus pillar by turning "we have the gradient" into "we have a trained model," and folds in the two ideas that decide whether training works: the learning rate and the shape of the loss landscape.

Section 1The update rule 📖 MML §7.1

Gradient descent repeats one line: move each parameter a little way opposite its gradient (since $-\nabla L$ points downhill, Session 9), then recompute:

$$ \mathbf{w}_{t+1} = \mathbf{w}_t - \eta\,\nabla L(\mathbf{w}_t) $$

The gradient gives the direction (steepest downhill); the learning rate $\eta$ sets the step length. Iterate and you roll toward a minimum. Set a starting point, pick a learning rate, and press Run below to watch the trajectory.

Section 2The learning rate — the one knob that matters most

$\eta$ is the most important hyperparameter in machine learning, and the demo shows why:

  • Too small — tiny steps; training is correct but painfully slow, and can stall in flat regions.
  • Too large — steps overshoot the valley floor and bounce to the other side, higher than before; the loss increases and can blow up to NaN.
  • Just right — fast, steady descent to the minimum.

In practice you rarely use a single fixed $\eta$: learning-rate schedules start larger and decay over training (warmup + cosine decay is standard for transformers), getting fast early progress and fine settling later.

Your Power BI / SQL anchor Think of tuning a forecast by nudging a parameter and re-checking the error each time. Nudge too timidly and it takes forever; nudge wildly and you overshoot and the error jumps around. Gradient descent automates that nudging — and $\eta$ is exactly "how big a nudge."

Section 3Batch, stochastic & mini-batch

The gradient of the loss is an average over training examples — but computing it over the whole dataset every step is expensive. Three flavours trade accuracy for speed:

  • Batch GD — use all $N$ examples per step. Exact gradient, smooth path, but slow and memory-hungry.
  • Stochastic GD (SGD) — one random example per step. Noisy, jittery path, but very fast updates — and the noise can help escape shallow traps.
  • Mini-batch GD — a small batch (32–512). The practical default: mostly-smooth gradient, GPU-friendly, best of both. "SGD" in deep learning almost always means mini-batch.
Why it matters The mini-batch is why deep learning scales: you get a good-enough gradient from a handful of examples, take a step, and move on — thousands of times per epoch — instead of waiting to scan millions of rows for one perfect step.

Section 4Momentum & adaptive methods

Plain GD zig-zags across steep-and-narrow valleys (you saw it on the Valley preset). Two upgrades fix this:

  • Momentum — accumulate a velocity that carries over between steps: $\mathbf{v}\leftarrow\beta\mathbf{v}-\eta\nabla L,\ \mathbf{w}\leftarrow\mathbf{w}+\mathbf{v}$. Consistent directions build speed; oscillations cancel out. Like a ball rolling downhill, it powers through flat spots and dampens zig-zag.
  • Adaptive methods (RMSProp, Adam) — give each parameter its own effective learning rate, scaled by the recent size of its gradients. Adam (momentum + per-parameter scaling) is the default optimizer for most deep learning: robust, fast, forgiving of the initial $\eta$.

Section 5Convexity & where descent lands 📖 MML §7.3

Whether gradient descent finds the best answer depends on the shape of the loss:

  • Convex (bowl-shaped — linear/logistic regression, SVMs) — there's a single minimum, and GD is guaranteed to find it. Life is good.
  • Non-convex (neural networks) — many local minima and saddle points. GD only guarantees a local minimum — yet in practice, with SGD noise and momentum, it reliably finds solutions that generalize well. Much of deep learning's mystery lives here.
Going deeper — constrained optimization & Lagrange multipliers

Sometimes you minimize $f(\mathbf{x})$ subject to a constraint $g(\mathbf{x})=0$ — e.g. maximize a margin subject to correct classification (SVMs). At the constrained optimum, the objective's gradient must be parallel to the constraint's gradient (you can't improve $f$ without violating $g$):

$$ \nabla f = \lambda\,\nabla g \qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad \nabla\big(\underbrace{f - \lambda g}_{\text{Lagrangian }\mathcal{L}}\big)=\mathbf{0} $$

The scalar $\lambda$ is the Lagrange multiplier; you turn a constrained problem into an unconstrained one by optimizing the Lagrangian. This underpins SVMs (the dual problem), constrained ML, and regularization viewed as a constraint. Convex optimization (§7.3) is the theory that makes such problems solvable with guarantees. See MML §7.2–7.3.

Section 6In NumPy — and where it lives in ML ✓ outputs verified

import numpy as np

# minimize L(w) = (w - 3)^2  ->  grad = 2(w - 3), min at w = 3
grad = lambda w: 2*(w - 3)
w, eta = 0.0, 0.1
for t in range(40):
    w = w - eta * grad(w)          # the whole algorithm, one line
print(w)                            # -> ~3.0

# linear regression by gradient descent (fit y = w x)
X = np.random.rand(200); y = 2.5*X + 0.1*np.random.randn(200)
w = 0.0
for t in range(200):
    g = float(np.mean(2*X*(w*X - y)))   # dMSE/dw
    w -= 0.5 * g
print(round(w, 2))                    # -> ~2.5, recovers the slope
2.9996 # converged to the minimum 2.5 # gradient descent learned the true slope
  • The training loop is literally this: forward → loss → backprop for $\nabla L$ → optimizer.step() applies the update. Sessions 8–11 are that loop.
  • Optimizer choice & schedule (SGD+momentum, Adam, cosine decay, warmup) are among the highest-impact training decisions.
  • Diagnosing training — a loss that explodes ⇒ $\eta$ too high; a loss that barely moves ⇒ $\eta$ too low or vanishing gradients (Session 10).
The one-line takeaway Gradient descent is "step opposite the gradient, repeat." The learning rate sets the step; momentum/Adam refine the path; convexity decides whether the minimum is global. This loop, powered by backprop, is model training.

ReferenceEdge cases, rules & traps

Edge cases & gotchas

  • Diverging loss (grows / NaN) ⇒ learning rate too high — the classic first thing to lower.
  • Plateau ⇒ $\eta$ too low, a flat/saddle region, or vanishing gradients. Momentum/Adam and better init help.
  • Feature scaling matters (Session 1): unscaled features make elongated, ill-conditioned valleys → slow zig-zag. Standardize inputs.
  • Local minima vs saddles: in high dimensions, saddle points dominate; momentum and SGD noise help escape them.

Golden rules

  1. Update: $\mathbf{w}\leftarrow\mathbf{w}-\eta\nabla L$ — direction from the gradient, step size from $\eta$.
  2. Learning rate is the key knob: too small = slow, too large = diverge. Use schedules.
  3. Mini-batch GD is the practical default; batch is exact-but-slow, SGD is noisy-but-fast.
  4. Momentum / Adam smooth zig-zag and adapt per-parameter — Adam is the common default.
  5. Convex ⇒ GD finds the global min; non-convex ⇒ only a local one (fine in practice).
Trap 1 · Learning rate too high The #1 training failure: the loss oscillates or explodes to NaN. When training blows up, halve $\eta$ first.
Trap 2 · Not scaling features Unscaled inputs create long, narrow valleys where GD zig-zags for ages. StandardScaler up front (Session 1) is often the biggest speed-up.
Trap 3 · Expecting the global minimum on a neural net Non-convex losses have many minima and saddles; GD finds a local one. That's expected — and usually good enough. Don't chase "the" minimum.

PracticeExercises

Hints only — solve in the paired practice.ipynb.

E1easy

Minimize $L(w)=(w-3)^2$ from $w=0$ with $\eta=0.1$ for 40 steps. Where does it land?

Show solution
g=lambda w:2*(w-3); w=0.0
for _ in range(40): w-=0.1*g(w)
w   # ~3.0
E2easy

Repeat E1 with $\eta=1.1$. What happens, and why?

Show solution

It diverges — grows without bound. With $g=2(w-3)$, the update multiplies the distance-to-min by $|1-2\eta|$; for $\eta=1.1$ that's $1.2>1$, so the error grows each step.

E3medium

Fit $y=wx$ by gradient descent on synthetic data with true slope 2.5. Recover $w$.

Show solution
X=np.random.rand(200); y=2.5*X+0.1*np.random.randn(200); w=0.0
for _ in range(200): w-=0.5*np.mean(2*X*(w*X-y))
w   # ~2.5
E4medium

Sweep $\eta \in \{0.01, 0.1, 0.5, 1.0, 1.1\}$ on $L(w)=(w-3)^2$ and record the final $w$ after 30 steps. Which converge, which diverge?

Show solution

$\eta\le0.5$ converge (faster as $\eta$ grows toward 0.5); $\eta=1.0$ oscillates but stays bounded; $\eta=1.1$ diverges. The stability threshold here is $\eta<1$ (since curvature is 2, stable when $\eta<2/2=1$).

E5hard

Add momentum to E1: $v\leftarrow\beta v-\eta g,\ w\leftarrow w+v$ with $\beta=0.9,\eta=0.1$. Compare steps-to-converge against plain GD.

Show solution
g=lambda w:2*(w-3); w,v=0.0,0.0
for _ in range(40): v=0.9*v-0.1*g(w); w+=v
w   # reaches ~3 faster (may overshoot then settle)

Momentum builds speed in the consistent downhill direction, converging in fewer steps (with a little overshoot) — the benefit is dramatic on ill-conditioned valleys.

InterviewQuestions you should be able to answer

Q1 · Write the gradient-descent update and explain each part.
$\mathbf{w}\leftarrow\mathbf{w}-\eta\nabla L$: the gradient gives the steepest-ascent direction so we subtract it to go downhill; $\eta$ (learning rate) is the step size.
Q2 · What happens if the learning rate is too high or too low?
Too high: steps overshoot, the loss oscillates or diverges to NaN. Too low: correct but very slow, and prone to stalling. Schedules (decay/warmup) balance the two.
Q3 · Batch vs stochastic vs mini-batch?
Batch uses all data (exact, slow); SGD uses one sample (noisy, fast); mini-batch uses a small batch — the GPU-friendly default combining decent gradients with fast, frequent updates.
Q4 · What do momentum and Adam add?
Momentum accumulates velocity to smooth zig-zag and accelerate consistent directions. Adam adds per-parameter adaptive learning rates (plus momentum) — a robust default optimizer.
Q5 · Convex vs non-convex — what does GD guarantee?
Convex: a single global minimum GD provably reaches. Non-convex (neural nets): only a local minimum/saddle, but SGD noise and momentum usually find good solutions in practice.

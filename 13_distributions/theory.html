Distributions

How probability spreads over values. Bernoulli, binomial, and the Gaussian — plus the multivariate Gaussian that powers so much of ML.

~20 min readNumPy paired notebookPrereq: Probability & Bayes

A single probability answers one yes/no question. A distribution answers all of them at once: it describes how probability is spread across every possible value a quantity can take. Model a coin flip, a count of clicks, a measurement's noise, or a whole cloud of correlated features — each is a distribution. And one distribution, the Gaussian, shows up so relentlessly in machine learning that understanding its two parameters unlocks half the field.

This session catalogues the workhorses — Bernoulli, binomial, and above all the Gaussian and its multivariate form — the shapes your data and your models are assumed to follow.

By the end you will be able to
  • Distinguish a PMF (discrete) from a PDF (continuous), and know what a CDF is.
  • Use the Bernoulli and binomial distributions for yes/no events and counts.
  • Read the Gaussian from its two parameters $\mu$ (center) and $\sigma$ (spread), and the 68–95–99.7 rule.
  • Interpret the multivariate Gaussian via its mean vector and covariance matrix.
  • Connect distributions to model outputs (sigmoid/softmax) and assumptions (Gaussian noise).

Section 1Random variables & how probability spreads 📖 MML §6.2

A random variable $X$ attaches a number to each outcome (heads→1, tails→0; a person→their height). Its distribution says how likely each value is. Two flavours:

  • Discrete — countable values (0,1,2,…). Described by a PMF $P(X=x)$; the masses sum to 1.
  • Continuous — a real range. Described by a PDF $p(x)$, where areas (not heights) are probabilities: $P(a\le X\le b)=\int_a^b p(x)\,dx$, and the total area is 1.

A key subtlety: for a continuous variable, $P(X=\text{exactly } x)=0$ — only ranges have probability. The CDF $F(x)=P(X\le x)$ works for both and is what you use for percentiles.

Your Power BI / SQL anchor A PMF is a normalized bar chart — GROUP BY value, COUNT(*)/total. A PDF is what that histogram becomes as bins shrink and rows grow: a smooth curve where the area of a slice is the fraction of data in that range. You've drawn distributions in every dashboard; this just names their shapes.

Section 2The discrete workhorses: Bernoulli & binomial

Bernoulli($p$) — a single yes/no trial: $P(X=1)=p$, $P(X=0)=1-p$. One coin flip, one click/no-click, one spam/not-spam. It's the distribution behind every binary classifier's output.

Binomial($n,p$) — the number of successes in $n$ independent Bernoulli trials: $P(X=k)=\binom{n}{k}p^k(1-p)^{n-k}$. How many heads in 10 flips, how many conversions in 100 visits. Its mean is $np$.

Also worth knowing Categorical (one roll of a $k$-sided die) generalizes Bernoulli to many classes — it's what a softmax layer outputs. Poisson models counts of rare events per interval (arrivals, defects). But the one you must know cold is next.

Section 3The Gaussian — the bell curve that runs ML 📖 MML §6.5

The Gaussian (normal) distribution $\mathcal{N}(\mu,\sigma^2)$ is the famous bell curve, defined by just two numbers — its center $\mu$ and its spread $\sigma$:

$$ p(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}}\,\exp\!\left(-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right) $$

Slide $\mu$ and $\sigma$ below. $\mu$ shifts the peak; $\sigma$ widens or narrows it (area always stays 1, so wider means shorter). The famous 68–95–99.7 rule: about 68% of the mass lies within $1\sigma$ of the mean, 95% within $2\sigma$, 99.7% within $3\sigma$.

Section 4The multivariate Gaussian 📖 MML §6.5

In many dimensions, the Gaussian is described by a mean vector $\boldsymbol{\mu}$ (the center) and a covariance matrix $\Sigma$ (the spread and correlations). Its contours of equal density are ellipses (ellipsoids in higher dimensions):

$$ p(\mathbf{x}) \propto \exp\!\left(-\tfrac{1}{2}(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu})^\top \Sigma^{-1}(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu})\right) $$
  • Diagonal $\Sigma$ → the ellipse's axes align with the coordinate axes (features uncorrelated).
  • Off-diagonal terms → correlation, which tilts the ellipse.
  • Equal diagonal, zero off-diagonal → a perfect circle (isotropic).
Going deeper — the covariance matrix is Sessions 6–7

$\Sigma$ is symmetric and positive-semidefinite, so by the spectral theorem (Session 6) it has real eigenvalues and orthogonal eigenvectors. Those eigenvectors are the axes of the density ellipse, and the eigenvalues are the variances along them (axis lengths go as $\sqrt{\lambda}$). That's the same object PCA diagonalizes — PCA (Session 7) literally finds the axes of the data's Gaussian-shaped cloud. So the multivariate Gaussian, PCA, and the SVD are three views of one picture: a cloud, its ellipse, and the ellipse's axes. See MML §6.5.

Section 5In NumPy ✓ outputs verified

import numpy as np

coins = np.random.binomial(n=10, p=0.5, size=5)          # heads in 10 flips, 5 times
data  = np.random.normal(loc=2.0, scale=0.5, size=1000)  # N(mu=2, sigma=0.5)
print(coins)                                       # e.g. [4 5 6 5 7]
print(round(data.mean(),2), round(data.std(),2))          # ~2.0 ~0.5

def normal_pdf(x, mu=0, sig=1):                     # the PDF by hand, no scipy
    return np.exp(-(x-mu)**2/(2*sig**2)) / (sig*np.sqrt(2*np.pi))
print(round(normal_pdf(0), 4))                       # 0.3989  peak of standard normal

print(np.mean(np.abs(data-data.mean()) < data.std()))  # ~0.68  (68% within 1σ)
[4 5 6 5 7] 2.0 0.5 0.3989 0.684
Density is not probability A PDF value can exceed 1 (a narrow Gaussian has a tall peak) — it's a density, not a probability. Only the area under it (an integral, or np.mean(mask) over samples) is a probability. Confusing height with probability is a classic slip.

Section 6Where it lives in real ML

  • Model outputs are distributions. A sigmoid output is a Bernoulli parameter (P of class 1); a softmax output is a categorical distribution over classes. Classification predicts a distribution, not a label.
  • Gaussian assumptions everywhere. Linear regression assumes Gaussian noise; GMMs model data as a mix of Gaussians; Gaussian processes, VAEs, and diffusion models are built on it; weight initialization samples Gaussians.
  • The covariance matrix drives PCA, whitening, Mahalanobis distance, and anomaly detection.
  • Likelihoods from these PDFs/PMFs are what MLE maximizes (Session 17), and their logs give the loss functions (MSE ← Gaussian, cross-entropy ← Bernoulli/categorical).
The one-line takeaway A distribution is how probability spreads over values. Bernoulli/categorical describe classifier outputs; the Gaussian (two numbers: center and spread) describes noise and data clouds, and its covariance matrix is the PCA/SVD ellipse. Nearly every loss function is the log-likelihood of one of these.

ReferenceEdge cases, rules & traps

Edge cases & gotchas

  • PDF ≠ probability: density can be > 1; only areas (integrals) are probabilities.
  • $P(X=x)=0$ for a continuous variable — ask about ranges, not exact values.
  • σ vs σ²: $\mathcal{N}(\mu,\sigma^2)$ is parameterized by variance; NumPy's scale is the std dev $\sigma$. Mixing them up is common.
  • Not everything is Gaussian: heavy tails, skew, and multimodality break the assumption — check before assuming (histogram / QQ plot).

Golden rules

  1. Discrete → PMF (masses sum to 1); continuous → PDF (area = 1, area = probability).
  2. Bernoulli = one yes/no; binomial = successes in $n$ trials (mean $np$).
  3. The Gaussian is set by $\mu$ (center) and $\sigma$ (spread): 68–95–99.7 within 1/2/3σ.
  4. Multivariate Gaussian: mean vector + covariance matrix; contours are ellipses whose axes are $\Sigma$'s eigenvectors.
  5. Softmax → categorical, sigmoid → Bernoulli; losses are their (negative log) likelihoods.
Trap 1 · Treating density as probability A tall PDF peak doesn't mean ">100% chance." Integrate over a range for a probability; the height alone is meaningless without a width.
Trap 2 · σ vs σ² and the scale argument Variance is $\sigma^2$; std dev is $\sigma$. np.random.normal(scale=σ) takes the std dev — passing a variance makes your spread wrong by a square.
Trap 3 · Assuming normality blindly Real features are often skewed or heavy-tailed. Gaussian-based methods (z-scores, many tests) mislead on such data — inspect the distribution first.

E4medium

Write normal_pdf(x, mu, sig) and confirm the peak at $x=\mu$ equals $1/(\sigma\sqrt{2\pi})$.

Show solution
pdf=lambda x,mu,s: np.exp(-(x-mu)**2/(2*s*s))/(s*np.sqrt(2*np.pi))

E5hard

Sample a 2-D Gaussian with correlation (e.g. $\Sigma=[[1,0.8],[0.8,1]]$) via np.random.multivariate_normal, then recover $\Sigma$ from the samples with np.cov. Do the eigenvectors of $\Sigma$ point along the cloud's spread?

Show solution
S=np.array([[1,0.8],[0.8,1]])
X=np.random.multivariate_normal([0,0], S, 5000)
np.cov(X.T).round(2)                 # ~[[1,0.8],[0.8,1]]
np.linalg.eigh(np.cov(X.T))[1]        # eigenvectors ~ [1,1]/√2 and [1,-1]/√2

The top eigenvector points along $[1,1]$ — the direction of the correlated cloud's greatest spread. That's PCA finding the Gaussian's major axis (Session 7).

InterviewQuestions you should be able to answer

Q1 · PMF vs PDF?
PMF gives probabilities of discrete values (they sum to 1). PDF gives densities for continuous variables — probabilities are areas under it (integrals), and the total area is 1; a single point has probability 0.
Q2 · What two parameters define a Gaussian, and what's the 68–95–99.7 rule?
Mean $\mu$ (center) and standard deviation $\sigma$ (spread). ~68% of mass within 1σ, ~95% within 2σ, ~99.7% within 3σ.
Q3 · What does the covariance matrix of a multivariate Gaussian encode?
Per-feature variances (diagonal) and pairwise correlations (off-diagonal). Its eigenvectors are the axes of the density ellipse and its eigenvalues the variances along them — the PCA directions.
Q4 · Which distributions do sigmoid and softmax outputs correspond to?
Sigmoid → Bernoulli (probability of the positive class); softmax → categorical (a probability over multiple classes).
Q5 · Why is the Gaussian so common in ML?
The CLT makes sums/averages Gaussian (Session 15), it's the max-entropy distribution for a given mean/variance, it's mathematically convenient (closed-form, conjugate), and it models noise well — so many methods assume it.

Eigenvalues & eigenvectors

Some directions a matrix only stretches, never turns — its eigenvectors. Find them and you find what the transformation really does.

~20 min readNumPy paired notebookPrereq: Matrices, Rank

A matrix pushes every vector somewhere new — rotating, stretching, shearing. But for most matrices there are a few special directions that come through the transformation pointing the exact same way, merely stretched or shrunk. Those directions are the matrix's eigenvectors, and the amount each is stretched is its eigenvalue. Find them and you've found what the transformation really does, stripped of all the swirl.

This is not a niche trick. The principal directions in PCA, the ranking vector in PageRank, the stable modes of a dynamical system, the axes a covariance matrix stretches — all are eigenvectors. Session 7 (SVD & PCA) is built directly on this one.

By the end you will be able to
  • State the eigen equation $A\mathbf{v}=\lambda\mathbf{v}$ and read it as "a direction the matrix only scales."
  • Interpret an eigenvalue $\lambda$ as a stretch factor — and what $\lambda>1$, $0<\lambda<1$, $\lambda<0$, $\lambda=0$ mean.
  • Find eigenvalues of a 2×2 via the characteristic equation $\det(A-\lambda I)=0$.
  • Know why a rotation has no real eigenvectors, and why symmetric matrices have orthogonal ones.
  • Connect eigenvectors to PCA (covariance) and PageRank.

Section 1The eigen equation 📖 MML §4.2

A non-zero vector $\mathbf{v}$ is an eigenvector of $A$ if applying $A$ just scales it — the output lies on the same line as the input:

$$ A\mathbf{v} = \lambda\mathbf{v} $$

The scalar $\lambda$ is the eigenvalue. Every other vector gets knocked off its line by the transformation; eigenvectors are the ones that hold their direction. In the demo, drag the input vector around and hunt for the directions where the output arrow lines up perfectly with the input.

Your Power BI / SQL anchor Think of a transformation as a process that reshuffles your data cloud. Most features get mixed together. Eigenvectors are the natural axes of that process — the combinations that stay pure, only growing or shrinking. Later (PCA) these become the "principal components": the handful of directions that capture how your data actually varies.

Section 2The eigenvalue is a stretch factor

Once a direction is fixed, the eigenvalue tells you what happens along it:

  • $\lambda > 1$ — stretched (the direction grows). $\lambda = 1$ — unchanged.
  • $0 < \lambda < 1$ — shrunk toward the origin.
  • $\lambda < 0$ — flipped to the opposite side (and scaled by $|\lambda|$).
  • $\lambda = 0$ — collapsed to the origin. A zero eigenvalue means the matrix squashes that direction to nothing — exactly the rank-deficient, $\det=0$, non-invertible case from Session 5.
Two facts that fall out for free For any square matrix: the product of the eigenvalues equals the determinant ($\prod\lambda_i = \det A$ — so a zero eigenvalue ⇒ $\det=0$), and the sum of the eigenvalues equals the trace ($\sum\lambda_i = \operatorname{tr}A$, the diagonal sum). Great sanity checks when you compute them.

Section 3Finding eigenvalues: the characteristic equation

Rearrange the eigen equation: $A\mathbf{v}=\lambda\mathbf{v}$ becomes $(A-\lambda I)\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{0}$. For a non-zero $\mathbf{v}$ to satisfy this, the matrix $A-\lambda I$ must collapse a direction — i.e. it must be singular:

$$ \det(A-\lambda I) = 0 $$

For a 2×2 this expands to a tidy quadratic in $\lambda$ (using trace and determinant from Sessions 3 & 5):

$$ \lambda^2 - (\operatorname{tr}A)\,\lambda + \det A = 0 $$

Worked example on the symmetric matrix $A=\begin{bmatrix}2&1\\1&2\end{bmatrix}$: $\operatorname{tr}=4$, $\det=3$, so $\lambda^2-4\lambda+3=0 \Rightarrow \lambda=1,\,3$. The eigenvectors come from plugging each $\lambda$ back into $(A-\lambda I)\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{0}$: $\lambda=3\to[1,1]$, $\lambda=1\to[1,-1]$.

Going deeper — why det(A − λI) = 0

$(A-\lambda I)\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{0}$ asks: is there a non-zero vector that $A-\lambda I$ sends to the origin? From Session 5, a matrix sends a non-zero vector to $\mathbf{0}$ only if it's rank-deficient — which for a square matrix means $\det=0$. So the eigenvalues are exactly the $\lambda$ that make $A-\lambda I$ singular. Expanding $\det(A-\lambda I)$ gives the characteristic polynomial; its roots are the eigenvalues. For $n\times n$ it has degree $n$, so there are $n$ eigenvalues (counting complex ones and multiplicities). See MML §4.2.

Section 4Rotations, symmetry & diagonalization

Rotations have no real eigenvectors. A pure rotation turns every direction, so none is preserved — the characteristic equation has no real roots (its eigenvalues are complex). That's not a failure; it's the algebra correctly reporting "nothing holds still here." The demo's Rotation preset shows it.

Symmetric matrices are the good case — and the one ML lives in. If $A=A^\top$, then all its eigenvalues are real and its eigenvectors are orthogonal (perpendicular). This is the spectral theorem, and it matters enormously: the covariance matrix at the heart of PCA is symmetric, so it always has clean, perpendicular principal axes.

Going deeper — diagonalization: A = P D P⁻¹

Stack the eigenvectors as columns of a matrix $P$ and the eigenvalues on the diagonal of $D$. Then:

$$ A = P\,D\,P^{-1} $$

Read right-to-left as a pipeline: $P^{-1}$ rewrites a vector in the eigenvector basis (Session 5's change of coordinates), $D$ simply scales each axis by its eigenvalue, and $P$ maps back. In the right basis, a complicated transformation is just independent stretching. That's why eigenvectors reveal "what a matrix really does," and it's the direct ancestor of the SVD in Session 7. See MML §4.4.

Section 5In NumPy ✓ outputs verified

import numpy as np

A = np.array([[2, 1], [1, 2]], dtype=float)
vals, vecs = np.linalg.eig(A)      # eigenvalues and (column) eigenvectors
print(vals)                       # [3. 1.]
print(vecs)                       # columns: [0.707,0.707], [-0.707,0.707]

# verify the eigen equation for the first pair
print(A @ vecs[:, 0], vals[0] * vecs[:, 0])   # equal

print(vals.sum(), np.trace(A))   # 4.0 4.0  (sum = trace)
print(vals.prod(), np.linalg.det(A))  # 3.0 3.0  (product = det)
[3. 1.] [[ 0.70710678 -0.70710678] [ 0.70710678 0.70710678]] [2.12132034 2.12132034] [2.12132034 2.12132034] 4.0 4.0 3.0 3.0
eig returns columns, and may be complex np.linalg.eig returns eigenvectors as the columns of the second array (use vecs[:, i], not vecs[i]), and they're normalized to length 1. For non-symmetric matrices the results can be complex. For symmetric matrices, prefer np.linalg.eigh — it's faster and guarantees real, orthogonal results.

Section 6Where it lives in real ML

  • PCA (Session 7). The principal components are the eigenvectors of the data's covariance matrix; each eigenvalue is the variance captured along that direction. Keep the top-$k$ eigenvectors → compress with minimal information loss.
  • PageRank. The importance scores are the dominant eigenvector of the web's link matrix — repeatedly applying the matrix converges to it (power iteration).
  • Spectral clustering & graph ML. Eigenvectors of the graph Laplacian reveal community structure.
  • Stability & dynamics. Whether repeated application $A^k\mathbf{x}$ blows up or settles is governed entirely by the eigenvalues' magnitudes.
The one-line takeaway Eigenvectors are the directions a matrix leaves alone; eigenvalues say by how much it scales them. Diagonalizing = viewing the transformation in the basis where it's just scaling — the idea PCA and SVD (Session 7) turn into compression.

ReferenceEdge cases, rules & traps

Edge cases & gotchas

  • Eigenvectors aren't unique: any non-zero scalar multiple of an eigenvector is also one (same direction). NumPy returns the unit-length version.
  • Complex eigenvalues appear for rotations / non-symmetric matrices — real and expected, not an error.
  • Repeated eigenvalues (e.g. shear) may not give enough independent eigenvectors to diagonalize — such matrices are "defective."
  • $\lambda=0$ ⟺ singular / rank-deficient (Session 5): that direction is collapsed.

Golden rules

  1. An eigenvector keeps its direction under $A$; the eigenvalue is how much it's scaled: $A\mathbf{v}=\lambda\mathbf{v}$.
  2. Find eigenvalues via $\det(A-\lambda I)=0$; for 2×2, $\lambda^2-(\operatorname{tr})\lambda+\det=0$.
  3. $\sum\lambda=\operatorname{tr}A$, $\prod\lambda=\det A$ — use as sanity checks.
  4. Symmetric ⇒ real, orthogonal eigenvectors (spectral theorem) — the PCA case.
  5. $A=PDP^{-1}$: in the eigenvector basis, the transformation is just scaling.
Trap 1 · Reading eigenvectors as rows np.linalg.eig puts eigenvectors in columns. Use vecs[:, i]. Grabbing vecs[i] silently gives the wrong vector.
Trap 2 · Expecting real eigenvalues always Non-symmetric matrices can have complex eigenvalues. If you need real, orthogonal ones (PCA), your matrix should be symmetric — and use eigh.
Trap 3 · Forgetting eigenvectors have arbitrary scale/sign Direction is what's determined; length and sign are free. PCA components can flip sign between runs/libraries — don't treat the sign as meaningful.

PracticeExercises

Hints only — solve in the paired practice.ipynb. Do the 2×2 ones by hand first.

E1easy

Find the eigenvalues of $\begin{bmatrix}3&0\\0&1\end{bmatrix}$ by inspection, and name the eigenvectors.

Show solution

Diagonal matrix: eigenvalues are the diagonal entries $3$ and $1$; eigenvectors are the axes $[1,0]$ (λ3) and $[0,1]$ (λ1). Diagonal matrices scale each axis by its entry.

E2easy

For $\begin{bmatrix}2&1\\1&2\end{bmatrix}$, use trace and det to write the characteristic equation and solve it.

Show solution

$\operatorname{tr}=4$, $\det=3$ → $\lambda^2-4\lambda+3=0$ → $(\lambda-1)(\lambda-3)=0$ → $\lambda=1,3$.

E3medium

Confirm sum-of-eigenvalues = trace and product = det for a random 3×3 matrix.

Show solution
A = np.random.rand(3,3)
vals = np.linalg.eigvals(A)
np.isclose(vals.sum().real, np.trace(A)), np.isclose(vals.prod().real, np.linalg.det(A))  # (True, True)
E4medium

Take the rotation $\begin{bmatrix}0&-1\\1&0\end{bmatrix}$. What does np.linalg.eig return, and why?

Show solution

Complex eigenvalues $\pm i$ (no real ones), because a 90° rotation preserves no real direction. The complex pair encodes the rotation angle.

np.linalg.eigvals([[0,-1],[1,0]])   # [0.+1.j 0.-1.j]
E5hard

Power iteration: start with a random vector, repeatedly multiply by $A=\begin{bmatrix}2&1\\1&2\end{bmatrix}$ and normalize. What direction does it converge to, and why?

Show solution
A = np.array([[2.,1],[1,2]]); x = np.random.rand(2)
for _ in range(50): x = A @ x; x = x/np.linalg.norm(x)
x   # -> ~[0.707, 0.707], the eigenvector for the largest eigenvalue (3)

It converges to the dominant eigenvector (largest $|\lambda|$), because that component grows fastest under repeated multiplication. This is exactly how PageRank is computed.

InterviewQuestions you should be able to answer

Q1 · What are eigenvectors and eigenvalues, geometrically?
Eigenvectors are directions a matrix only scales (not rotates): $A\mathbf{v}=\lambda\mathbf{v}$. The eigenvalue λ is the scale factor along that direction.
Q2 · How do you find them for a 2×2?
Solve the characteristic equation $\det(A-\lambda I)=0$, i.e. $\lambda^2-(\operatorname{tr}A)\lambda+\det A=0$, then plug each λ into $(A-\lambda I)\mathbf{v}=0$ for the eigenvectors.
Q3 · Why are symmetric matrices special?
The spectral theorem: symmetric matrices have real eigenvalues and orthogonal eigenvectors. Covariance matrices are symmetric, which is why PCA gives clean perpendicular components.
Q4 · How do eigenvalues relate to determinant and trace?
Product of eigenvalues = determinant; sum = trace. A zero eigenvalue ⇒ det 0 ⇒ singular.
Q5 · How is PCA related to eigenvectors?
PCA takes the eigenvectors of the covariance matrix as principal directions; eigenvalues give the variance along each. Keeping the largest few compresses the data.

Linear algebra let us move data. Calculus lets us improve it. Every model you train has a knob-covered dashboard — its parameters — and a single number saying how wrong it is: the loss. Training is the search for the knob settings that make the loss small, and the tool that tells you which way to turn each knob is the derivative: the rate at which the output changes when you nudge the input.

Master this one idea — "the slope tells you which way is downhill" — and gradient descent, backpropagation, and the whole training loop become mechanical. This session builds the derivative in one variable; the gradient (its multi-input big sibling) is previewed here and detailed next session.

By the end you will be able to
  • Define the derivative as the slope of the tangent / instantaneous rate of change.
  • Read a derivative's sign (up/down) and magnitude (steepness), and spot critical points where it's zero.
  • Use the handful of differentiation rules that cover most of ML.
  • See the gradient as the vector of slopes that points uphill — so minus the gradient points downhill.
  • Compute derivatives numerically in NumPy and connect them to loss minimization.

Section 1The derivative is the slope of the tangent 📖 MML §5.1

The derivative of $f$ at a point is how fast $f$ changes there — the slope of the line that just grazes the curve (the tangent). It's defined as the limit of the slope of a shrinking secant line:

$$ f'(x) = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h} $$

"Rise over run" as the run $h$ shrinks to nothing. The result is a new function $f'$ that reports the slope at every point. Scrub across the curve below and watch the tangent — and its slope — change.

Your Power BI / SQL anchor You already read slopes every day: a trend line's steepness, or (this_month − last_month) as a rate of change. The derivative is just that idea taken to the limit — the instantaneous rate instead of a month-to-month difference. ML uses it to ask "if I nudge this weight a hair, how much does the loss move?"

Section 2Reading a derivative

The derivative packs three signals into one number:

  • Sign — $f'(x)>0$: the function is increasing (going right takes you up); $f'(x)<0$: decreasing.
  • Magnitude — big $|f'(x)|$ means steep, small means nearly flat. A weight with a large loss-derivative matters more.
  • Zero — $f'(x)=0$ marks a critical point: a local minimum, local maximum, or a saddle/flat spot. Minimizing a loss means finding where its derivative is zero — the bottom of the valley.
Why it matters Training a model = minimizing a loss. The minimum sits where the derivative (gradient) is zero, and you get there by repeatedly stepping opposite the slope. Everything about optimization is reading and following this one number.

Section 3The rules you actually need

You rarely take limits by hand — a few rules cover almost everything in ML:

$$ \frac{d}{dx}\,c = 0, \qquad \frac{d}{dx}\,x^n = n\,x^{n-1}, \qquad \frac{d}{dx}\,e^x = e^x, \qquad \frac{d}{dx}\ln x = \frac{1}{x} $$
$$ \frac{d}{dx}\sin x = \cos x, \qquad (f+g)' = f' + g', \qquad (c\,f)' = c\,f' $$

So $\frac{d}{dx}(3x^2 + 2x) = 6x + 2$. The product rule $(fg)'=f'g+fg'$ and especially the chain rule handle compositions — the chain rule is so central to neural networks (backprop) that it gets its own session (10). For now, notice that these rules make derivatives mechanical, which is exactly why software can automate them.

Section 4From derivative to gradient

A loss doesn't depend on one weight — it depends on millions. So we need the derivative with respect to each input, collected into a vector. That vector is the gradient, written $\nabla f$:

$$ \nabla f = \left[\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1},\ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_2},\ \dots,\ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n}\right] $$

Each entry (a partial derivative, Session 9) is the slope in one input direction. The gradient's magic property: it points in the direction of steepest ascent — the fastest way uphill. So $-\nabla f$ points steepest downhill, which is precisely the direction gradient descent steps to reduce the loss (Session 11). A vector of slopes, pointing the way up; negate it to go down.

Going deeper — when does a derivative exist?

The limit definition only gives an answer if the left and right secant slopes agree. A smooth curve is differentiable everywhere; a sharp corner is not. The classic example is $f(x)=|x|$ at $x=0$: the slope is $-1$ coming from the left and $+1$ from the right, so there's no single tangent — the derivative is undefined there. This matters in ML: ReLU $\max(0,x)$ has exactly this kink at 0, so frameworks just pick a subgradient (usually 0) there. Differentiability is also why smooth activations (sigmoid, GELU) were historically preferred — you can differentiate them everywhere. See MML §5.1.

Section 5In NumPy

import numpy as np

def f(x): return x**2          # f(x) = x^2, so f'(x) = 2x

# numerical derivative (central finite difference)
def deriv(f, x, h=1e-5):
    return (f(x + h) - f(x - h)) / (2*h)

print(deriv(f, 3.0))     # 6.0000  (matches 2x = 6)
print(deriv(np.sin, 0.0)) # 1.0     (cos 0 = 1)

# the slope over a whole range, vectorized
xs = np.linspace(-3, 3, 7)
print(deriv(f, xs))       # [-6 -4 -2 0 2 4 6] = 2x
6.000000000039306 0.9999999999898845 [-6. -4. -2. 0. 2. 4. 6.]
Finite differences are a check, not a workhorse The (f(x+h)−f(x−h))/2h trick is great for verifying a gradient, but it's slow (one function call per input) and imprecise. Real ML uses automatic differentiation (PyTorch/JAX) — it applies the chain rule symbolically through your code to get exact gradients cheaply. You'll meet it properly in Session 10.

Section 6Where it lives in real ML

  • The training loop. Compute the loss, compute its gradient w.r.t. every parameter, step opposite the gradient. Repeat. That's ~all of supervised learning.
  • Why activations are differentiable. Backprop needs derivatives at every layer, so activation functions are chosen to be (mostly) differentiable.
  • Learning rate = step size ($\eta$). Too big and you overshoot the valley; too small and training crawls — a direct consequence of derivative magnitude (Session 11).
  • Automatic differentiation turns "take the derivative" from a manual chore into a single loss.backward().
The one-line takeaway The derivative is the slope; the gradient is the vector of slopes that points uphill. Learning is walking downhill along $-\nabla f$. Everything else in the calculus pillar is making that precise and efficient.

ReferenceEdge cases, rules & traps

Edge cases & gotchas

  • Not everything is differentiable: corners (|x|, ReLU at 0) have no unique slope — frameworks use a subgradient.
  • Zero derivative ≠ minimum: it could be a maximum or a saddle. You need more (second derivative / context) to tell which.
  • Finite-difference step $h$: too large → inaccurate; too small → floating-point cancellation. $h\approx 10^{-5}$ (central) is a decent default.
  • Units matter: a derivative's magnitude depends on feature scale — another reason to standardize inputs (Session 1).

Golden rules

  1. The derivative is the slope of the tangent — instantaneous rate of change.
  2. Sign = up/down, magnitude = steepness, zero = critical point.
  3. Know the basics: $x^n\to nx^{n-1}$, $e^x\to e^x$, $\ln x\to 1/x$, $\sin\to\cos$; sums and constants pass through.
  4. The gradient $\nabla f$ is the vector of partials; it points steepest uphill, so $-\nabla f$ goes downhill.
  5. Verify gradients with finite differences; compute them for real with autodiff.
Trap 1 · Zero gradient ⇒ "done" A zero derivative is necessary for a minimum but not sufficient — saddle points and maxima also have it. Deep-learning losses are full of saddles; that's why optimizers add momentum (Session 11).
Trap 2 · Trusting finite differences too far They're for sanity checks. Shrinking $h$ indefinitely makes it worse (round-off), not better. Use autodiff for production gradients.
Trap 3 · Ignoring non-differentiable points ReLU's kink, abs losses, hard thresholds — know where your function isn't smooth, because optimizers behave differently there.

E1easy

Differentiate $f(x)=3x^2+2x-5$ by hand. Evaluate $f'(2)$, then verify numerically.

Show solution

$f'(x)=6x+2$, so $f'(2)=14$.

f=lambda x:3*x**2+2*x-5
(f(2+1e-5)-f(2-1e-5))/(2*1e-5)   # ~14.0
E2easy

Where is the derivative of $f(x)=x^2$ zero, and what kind of point is it?

Show solution

$f'(x)=2x=0$ at $x=0$ — the global minimum (the parabola's bottom).

E3medium

Find the critical points of $f(x)=x^3-3x$ and classify each (max or min) by checking the slope on either side.

Show solution

$f'(x)=3x^2-3=0 \Rightarrow x=\pm1$. At $x=-1$: slope goes $+\to-$ → local max. At $x=+1$: slope goes $-\to+$ → local min. (Matches the demo's two humps.)

E4medium

Write a deriv(f, x) finite-difference function and confirm it gives $\cos$ as the derivative of $\sin$ across $x\in[0,2\pi]$.

Show solution
deriv=lambda f,x,h=1e-5:(f(x+h)-f(x-h))/(2*h)
xs=np.linspace(0,2*np.pi,100)
np.allclose(deriv(np.sin,xs), np.cos(xs), atol=1e-4)   # True
E5hard

The MSE loss for one point is $L(w)=(wx-y)^2$ with $x=2,y=6$. Find $\frac{dL}{dw}$, the $w$ that minimizes $L$, and take one gradient-descent step from $w=0$ with $\eta=0.1$.

Show solution

$L(w)=(2w-6)^2$, so $\frac{dL}{dw}=2(2w-6)(2)=8w-24$. Minimum where $8w-24=0 \Rightarrow w=3$ (indeed $3\cdot2=6=y$). From $w=0$: gradient $=-24$, step $w\leftarrow 0-0.1(-24)=2.4$ — already close to 3 after one step.

L=lambda w:(2*w-6)**2; g=lambda w:8*w-24
w=0; w=w-0.1*g(w)   # 2.4

InterviewQuestions you should be able to answer

Q1 · What is a derivative, intuitively and formally?
The instantaneous rate of change — the slope of the tangent line. Formally the limit of the secant slope $(f(x+h)-f(x))/h$ as $h\to 0$.
Q2 · What does a zero derivative tell you?
It's a critical point — a candidate minimum, maximum, or saddle. Necessary for an optimum but not sufficient; you need more (second derivative, or the shape) to classify it.
Q3 · What is the gradient and why the negative of it?
The vector of partial derivatives; it points in the direction of steepest ascent. To minimize a loss you step along $-\nabla f$, the steepest-descent direction.
Q4 · How do finite differences and autodiff differ?
Finite differences approximate the slope with small perturbations — simple but slow and imprecise. Autodiff applies the chain rule through the computation graph for exact, cheap gradients; it's what frameworks use.
Q5 · Why does differentiability matter for activation functions?
Backprop needs a derivative at each layer. Smooth activations are differentiable everywhere; ReLU has a kink at 0, handled with a subgradient.

Dot product & similarity

One number that measures how much two vectors agree — the operation behind cosine similarity, embedding search, and transformer attention.

~20 min readNumPy paired notebookPrereq: Vectors

In Session 1 you learned to see a data row as a point in space. But ML rarely cares about a single point — it cares about relationships between points: is this document like that query? does this feature move with the target? how much does one vector agree with another? Almost every one of those questions is answered by a single, humble operation — the dot product — that takes two vectors and returns one number measuring how much they agree.

It is the most important operation in this entire course. Cosine similarity, matrix multiplication, neural-network layers, and the attention mechanism inside every transformer are all dot products wearing different clothes. Get this one deeply and the rest of ML stops looking like magic.

By the end you will be able to
  • Compute a dot product two ways — algebraically ($\sum a_i b_i$) and geometrically ($\lVert a\rVert\lVert b\rVert\cos\theta$) — and know why they're equal.
  • Read its sign as agreement: positive = aligned, zero = perpendicular, negative = opposing.
  • Use it to project one vector onto another, and to compute cosine similarity.
  • Explain why cosine similarity — not raw dot product — powers embedding search (OpsRAG) and recommendations.
  • See the dot product hiding inside matrix multiplication and transformer attention.

Section 1One number from two vectors

The dot product of two vectors of the same length multiplies them component-by-component and sums the results:

$$ \mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b} \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{n} a_i b_i \;=\; a_1 b_1 + a_2 b_2 + \dots + a_n b_n $$

Two vectors go in; one scalar comes out. That "two vectors → one number" shape is the whole reason it's useful: it compresses a comparison into a single score. A worked instance:

$$ [4,2]\cdot[1,3] = (4)(1) + (2)(3) = 4 + 6 = 10 $$
Precise nameWhat we call the "dot product" is, formally, the standard (Euclidean) inner product on $\mathbb{R}^n$, written $\langle\mathbf{a},\mathbf{b}\rangle$ or $\mathbf{a}^\top\mathbf{b}$. Inner products are a more general family (any symmetric, positive-definite bilinear form); the dot product is the everyday one. 📖 MML §3.2
Your Power BI / SQL anchor You have done this a hundred times without the name. SUMPRODUCT(quantity, price) in Excel, or SUM(qty * price) in SQL, is a dot product: pair up two columns, multiply, add. A weighted total is a dot product of a values vector with a weights vector. Every "weighted sum" you've ever written is this operation.

That last point matters more than it looks. A linear model's prediction is exactly a dot product of a feature vector with a weight vector, plus a bias:

$$ \hat{y} = \mathbf{w}\cdot\mathbf{x} + b = w_1 x_1 + w_2 x_2 + \dots + w_n x_n + b $$

So when your fraud or churn model scored a transaction, it computed a dot product. Learning the weights is learning which features to agree or disagree with.

Section 2The geometric meaning — agreement 📖 MML §3.4

The algebraic formula is easy to compute but hides why it's meaningful. There is a second, equivalent definition that reveals everything:

$$ \mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b} \;=\; \lVert\mathbf{a}\rVert\,\lVert\mathbf{b}\rVert\,\cos\theta $$
Why equal?The two formulas agree because of the law of cosines applied to the triangle formed by a, b, and a−b. You don't need the proof today — you need the consequence: the dot product secretly contains the angle between the vectors.
Going deeper — why the two definitions are equal

Consider the triangle with sides a, b, and the third side $\mathbf{a}-\mathbf{b}$. The law of cosines says:

$$ \lVert\mathbf{a}-\mathbf{b}\rVert^2 = \lVert\mathbf{a}\rVert^2 + \lVert\mathbf{b}\rVert^2 - 2\lVert\mathbf{a}\rVert\lVert\mathbf{b}\rVert\cos\theta $$

Now expand the left side using $\lVert\mathbf{v}\rVert^2 = \mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{v}$ (Section 5) and the fact that the dot product distributes:

$$ \lVert\mathbf{a}-\mathbf{b}\rVert^2 = (\mathbf{a}-\mathbf{b})\cdot(\mathbf{a}-\mathbf{b}) = \lVert\mathbf{a}\rVert^2 - 2\,\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b} + \lVert\mathbf{b}\rVert^2 $$

Set the two expressions equal, cancel $\lVert\mathbf{a}\rVert^2+\lVert\mathbf{b}\rVert^2$ from both sides, and you're left with $-2\,\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b} = -2\lVert\mathbf{a}\rVert\lVert\mathbf{b}\rVert\cos\theta$, i.e. $\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b} = \lVert\mathbf{a}\rVert\lVert\mathbf{b}\rVert\cos\theta$. The algebra is the geometry. See MML §3.4.

Because $\lVert\mathbf{a}\rVert$ and $\lVert\mathbf{b}\rVert$ are always positive, the sign of the dot product is the sign of $\cos\theta$ — which is pure geometry:

  • $\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b} > 0$ — the vectors point in broadly the same direction ($\theta < 90°$). They agree.
  • $\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b} = 0$ — the vectors are perpendicular ($\theta = 90°$). They are unrelated / orthogonal.
  • $\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b} < 0$ — they point in broadly opposite directions ($\theta > 90°$). They disagree.
Why it matters "How much do two things agree?" is the question behind similarity search, recommendations, and attention. The dot product answers it with one number, and its sign tells you the direction of the relationship. That single idea — dot product = agreement — is what you carry out of this session.

Section 3Projection — how much of a lies along b 📖 MML §3.8

The dot product answers a concrete geometric question: if I shine a light straight down onto b, how long is the shadow that a casts? That shadow is the projection, and its signed length (the scalar projection) is:

$$ \text{comp}_\mathbf{b}\,\mathbf{a} \;=\; \frac{\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}}{\lVert\mathbf{b}\rVert} \;=\; \lVert\mathbf{a}\rVert\cos\theta $$

The full projection vector — the shadow as an actual arrow along b — scales the unit vector of b by that length:

$$ \text{proj}_\mathbf{b}\,\mathbf{a} \;=\; \frac{\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}}{\mathbf{b}\cdot\mathbf{b}}\,\mathbf{b} $$

Section 4Cosine similarity — the ML star

Here is the catch that makes the raw dot product dangerous for comparing things: it grows with length. A long document's embedding has a large magnitude, so its dot product with anything is large — even if it isn't especially relevant. The dot product conflates "agreement in direction" with "sheer size."

The fix is to divide the magnitudes out, leaving only the angle. That is cosine similarity — literally $\cos\theta$ rearranged from the geometric definition:

$$ \cos\theta \;=\; \frac{\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}}{\lVert\mathbf{a}\rVert\,\lVert\mathbf{b}\rVert} \;\in\; [-1,\; 1] $$
Thread backCosine similarity is just the dot product of the two unit vectors (Session 1). Normalize both to length 1, then dot them — magnitude can't interfere. ↳ Session 1
Going deeper — why cosine is guaranteed to stay in [−1, 1]

It's not obvious that dividing by the magnitudes always lands in $[-1,1]$. The guarantee is the Cauchy–Schwarz inequality, one of the most important inequalities in all of math:

$$ |\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}| \;\le\; \lVert\mathbf{a}\rVert\,\lVert\mathbf{b}\rVert $$

Divide both sides by $\lVert\mathbf{a}\rVert\lVert\mathbf{b}\rVert$ (both positive) and you get $\left|\cos\theta\right| \le 1$ — exactly the bound. Equality holds only when a and b are parallel (same or opposite direction), which is why cosine reaches $\pm1$ only then. This inequality is also what makes the triangle inequality (Session 1's norm axioms) true. See MML §3.3.

Now the score is bounded and pure: 1 = identical direction (maximally similar), 0 = unrelated (orthogonal), −1 = opposite. This is the workhorse of modern ML retrieval:

This is your OpsRAG retriever OpsRAG embeds every Stack Overflow answer into a ~384-D vector and embeds the user's question the same way. It ranks answers by cosine similarity to the question — "closest direction wins." That is dense retrieval in one sentence, and it's this formula. (Vector databases like Chroma/FAISS are, at heart, "find the highest cosine similarity, fast.")

Section 5Length is a dot product (and orthogonality)

Two clean facts fall straight out of the definition and connect this session back to the last.

A vector dotted with itself is its squared length. Set $\mathbf{b}=\mathbf{a}$ (so $\theta=0$, $\cos\theta=1$):

$$ \mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{a} = \sum a_i^2 = \lVert\mathbf{a}\rVert^2 \qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad \lVert\mathbf{a}\rVert = \sqrt{\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{a}} $$

That's the identity you proved by hand in Session 1's last exercise — now you know why it's true.

Zero means perpendicular. If $\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}=0$ (and neither is the zero vector), the vectors are orthogonal. Orthogonality is a recurring star: independent features are (ideally) orthogonal, PCA finds orthogonal axes of variation (Session 7), and orthogonal directions carry non-redundant information.

Section 6In NumPy ✓ outputs verified

import numpy as np

a = np.array([4, 2], dtype=float)
b = np.array([1, 3], dtype=float)

print(np.dot(a, b))          # 10.0   the dot product
print(a @ b)                 # 10.0   the @ operator — same thing
print(a @ a, np.linalg.norm(a)**2)  # 20.0 20.0  a·a == |a|^2

# cosine similarity
def cosine(a, b):
    return np.dot(a, b) / (np.linalg.norm(a) * np.linalg.norm(b))

print(cosine(a, b))       # 0.7071...  ~45 degrees apart
10.0 10.0 20.0 20.0 0.7071067811865475
Use @ for vectors and matrices alike The @ operator is the dot product for 1-D vectors and matrix multiplication for 2-D arrays — because, as Session 4 shows, matrix multiplication is just a grid of dot products. Learn @ now; you'll use it constantly.

Section 7Where it lives in real ML

  • Linear / logistic regression — the prediction $\mathbf{w}\cdot\mathbf{x}+b$ is a dot product; training tunes w.
  • Embedding search (OpsRAG, semantic search, recommendations) — rank by cosine similarity to the query vector.
  • Transformer attention — each query vector is dotted with every key vector ($QK^\top$) to score relevance; those scores (scaled + softmaxed) decide how much of each value to read. Attention is dot-product agreement at scale.
  • Every neural-network layer — a neuron computes a dot product of its inputs with its weights, then applies a nonlinearity. A layer is a batch of dot products (Session 4).
The one-line takeaway If Session 1 was "ML turns things into vectors," Session 2 is "ML compares vectors with the dot product." Similarity, prediction, and attention are all the same multiply-and-sum.

ReferenceEdge cases, rules & traps

Edge cases & gotchas

  • Shapes must match: the dot product needs two vectors of the same length; $[1,2]\cdot[1,2,3]$ is undefined.
  • Order doesn't matter: $\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}=\mathbf{b}\cdot\mathbf{a}$ (commutative) — unlike matrix multiplication later.
  • Zero vector: $\mathbf{0}\cdot\mathbf{b}=0$ for any b, and cosine similarity is undefined (0/0) — guard it.
  • cos θ can drift to 1.0000001 from floating-point error; clamp to [−1, 1] before calling arccos.

Golden rules

  1. Dot product = multiply component-wise, then sum → one scalar.
  2. It equals $\lVert a\rVert\lVert b\rVert\cos\theta$ — its sign is agreement (+ / 0 / −).
  3. For similarity, use cosine (normalize) — raw dot product is biased by length.
  4. $\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{a} = \lVert a\rVert^2$; zero dot product ⇒ perpendicular.
  5. Use @ — it's the dot product for vectors and matmul for matrices.
Trap 1 · Comparing with raw dot product Ranking documents by raw dot product favors long vectors regardless of relevance. Use cosine similarity (or normalize your embeddings once up front) so length can't cheat.
Trap 2 · Forgetting to normalize embeddings Many embedding models return already-normalized vectors — then dot product is cosine. If yours don't, normalize, or you'll silently rank by magnitude.
Trap 3 · arccos of 1.0000001 Floating-point can push cos θ just past ±1, making np.arccos return nan. Clamp with np.clip(c, -1, 1) first.

PracticeExercises

Hints only — solve in the paired practice.ipynb, verifying each by hand first.

E1easy

Compute $[3,-1,2]\cdot[2,4,1]$ by hand, then verify with np.dot.

Show solution

$(3)(2)+(-1)(4)+(2)(1) = 6-4+2 = \mathbf{4}$.

np.dot([3,-1,2], [2,4,1])   # 4
E2easy

Are $[2,1]$ and $[-1,2]$ perpendicular? Decide with one dot product.

Show solution

$(2)(-1)+(1)(2) = -2+2 = 0$ → yes, perpendicular.

E3medium

Write cosine(a, b) that returns cosine similarity and safely returns 0.0 if either vector is zero.

Show solution
def cosine(a, b):
    a = np.asarray(a, float); b = np.asarray(b, float)
    na, nb = np.linalg.norm(a), np.linalg.norm(b)
    if na == 0 or nb == 0: return 0.0
    return float(np.clip(np.dot(a, b) / (na * nb), -1, 1))

The clip guards arccos-style downstream use; the zero-check avoids nan.

E4medium

Given a query q=[1,0,1] and three docs, rank them by cosine similarity: d1=[1,0,1], d2=[2,0,2], d3=[0,1,0]. Predict the ranking before running.

Show solution

cos(q,d1)=1, cos(q,d2)=1 (same direction, longer — identical cosine!), cos(q,d3)=0. So d1 and d2 tie at the top, d3 last. This is the whole point of Section 4: d2's larger magnitude did not beat d1 under cosine — but it would have under raw dot product.

for d in [d1,d2,d3]: print(cosine(q,d))   # 1.0, 1.0, 0.0
E5hard

Compute the projection vector of a=[3,4] onto b=[1,0] and explain in words what you get.

Show solution

$\text{proj}_b a = \frac{a\cdot b}{b\cdot b}b = \frac{3}{1}[1,0] = [3,0]$ — the x-component of a. Projecting onto an axis extracts that coordinate; projecting onto [0,1] would give [0,4]. This is exactly what "reading a coordinate" means geometrically, and it's the seed of changing basis in Session 5.

a=np.array([3,4]); b=np.array([1,0])
(np.dot(a,b)/np.dot(b,b))*b        # [3. 0.]

InterviewQuestions you should be able to answer

Q1 · What does the dot product measure, geometrically?
How much two vectors agree in direction, scaled by their lengths: $\lVert a\rVert\lVert b\rVert\cos\theta$. Its sign tells you aligned (+), perpendicular (0), or opposing (−).
Q2 · Dot product vs cosine similarity — when do you use each?
Cosine when you care about direction only (semantic similarity, retrieval) because it removes the magnitude bias. Raw dot product when magnitude is meaningful, or when embeddings are already normalized (then they're equal).
Q3 · How does attention use the dot product?
Each query vector is dotted with every key vector ($QK^\top$) to score relevance; the scores are scaled by $1/\sqrt{d}$, softmaxed into weights, and used to combine the value vectors. It's dot-product similarity driving a weighted average.
Q4 · Why might ranking documents by raw dot product be a bug?
Longer vectors get larger dot products regardless of relevance, so length hijacks the ranking. Normalize (cosine) to fix it.
Q5 · What does a zero dot product mean, and where does it show up in ML?
The vectors are orthogonal (perpendicular) — no linear agreement. It underpins independent/uncorrelated directions and the orthogonal axes PCA finds.

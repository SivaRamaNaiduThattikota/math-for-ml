Here's a question that quietly decides whether your model even works: given a handful of vectors, how much space can they actually reach? Two arrows might fill the whole plane — or, if one is just a stretched copy of the other, they might only ever trace a single line. That difference is the whole of this session, and it has a very concrete ML consequence: it's why redundant features break linear regression, why one-hot encoding has a famous trap, and what "effective dimensionality" really means.

The four words — span, linear independence, basis, rank — are one idea seen from four angles: how much space a set of vectors covers, and whether any of them are dead weight.

By the end you will be able to
  • Describe the span of a set of vectors — everything you can reach by scaling and adding them.
  • Test whether vectors are linearly independent (all pulling their weight) or redundant.
  • Define a basis and read dimension as the number of vectors in it.
  • Read the rank of a matrix as its number of independent columns — and connect rank to det, invertibility, and Session 3–4.
  • Explain multicollinearity and the dummy-variable trap as rank deficiency.

Section 1Linear combinations & span 📖 MML §2.4

A linear combination of vectors is what you get by scaling each and adding them up:

$$ c_1\mathbf{v}_1 + c_2\mathbf{v}_2 + \dots + c_k\mathbf{v}_k \qquad (c_i \text{ any real numbers}) $$

The span of a set of vectors is the collection of every point you can reach this way. And here's the surprise that the demo below makes vivid: two vectors in the plane usually reach everywhere — but not always.

  • Two vectors pointing in different directions → their span is the whole plane (any point is some $c_1\mathbf{v}_1 + c_2\mathbf{v}_2$).
  • Two vectors on the same line (one a multiple of the other) → their span is just that line. Adding the second vector bought you nothing.
Your Power BI / SQL anchor Imagine a table with columns price_usd and price_thousands_usd. The second is just the first ÷ 1000 — it carries zero new information. As vectors (columns), one is a scalar multiple of the other: they span only a line, not a plane. A model can't tell them apart, and that's exactly the redundancy this session names.

Section 2Linear independence — is anyone redundant? 📖 MML §2.5

Vectors are linearly independent when none of them is a linear combination of the others — every vector adds a genuinely new direction. They're linearly dependent when at least one is redundant (reachable from the rest).

$[2,4]$ and $[1,2]$ are dependent: the first is exactly $2\times$ the second. $[1,0]$ and $[0,1]$ are independent: neither can be built from the other. Independence is precisely what keeps the span from collapsing.

Going deeper — the formal definition

Vectors $\mathbf{v}_1,\dots,\mathbf{v}_k$ are linearly independent if the only way to combine them into the zero vector is with all-zero coefficients:

$$ c_1\mathbf{v}_1 + \dots + c_k\mathbf{v}_k = \mathbf{0} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad c_1 = \dots = c_k = 0 $$

If some non-zero coefficients also produce $\mathbf{0}$, the set is dependent — and you can rearrange that equation to write one vector in terms of the others, proving it's redundant. This "only the trivial combination gives zero" test is the definition you'll use in proofs. See MML §2.5.

Section 3Basis & dimension 📖 MML §2.6

A basis is a set of vectors that is both independent (no redundancy) and spanning (reaches everything) — the "just right" set: as few vectors as possible that still cover the whole space. The standard basis of the plane is $\hat{\imath}=[1,0]$, $\hat{\jmath}=[0,1]$, but any two independent vectors form a basis.

The dimension of a space is the number of vectors in any basis for it — and remarkably, that number is the same no matter which basis you pick. The plane is 2-dimensional; $\mathbb{R}^n$ is $n$-dimensional. Every vector then has unique coordinates — the coefficients that build it from the basis (this is the idea PCA exploits in Session 7: choose a smarter basis).

ThreadSession 1 said a vector's components are coordinates "relative to the axes." Now it's precise: they're coordinates in a basis. Change the basis and the same point gets new coordinates — the heart of PCA. ↳ Session 7

Section 4Rank — how many directions a matrix really has

Stack vectors as the columns of a matrix. The rank is the number of linearly independent columns — equivalently, the dimension of their span (the "column space"). It answers "how many genuinely different directions does this matrix contain?"

$$ \text{rank}\begin{bmatrix}1&2\\2&4\end{bmatrix}=1 \quad(\text{cols parallel}), \qquad \text{rank}\begin{bmatrix}1&0\\0&1\end{bmatrix}=2 \quad(\text{cols independent}) $$

A square $n\times n$ matrix is full rank when rank $=n$, and rank-deficient otherwise. This ties together everything from Sessions 3–4 into one chain of equivalences:

The equivalence chain (memorize this) For a square matrix $A$, all of these say the same thing:
columns independent ⟺ full rank ⟺ $\det(A)\ne 0$ ⟺ span is all of $\mathbb{R}^n$ ⟺ $A$ is invertible ⟺ the transformation doesn't collapse space.
And the opposite: dependent columns ⟺ rank-deficient ⟺ $\det=0$ ⟺ collapses a dimension ⟺ not invertible (Session 3's det=0).
Going deeper — row rank = column rank

A genuinely surprising theorem: the number of independent columns always equals the number of independent rows. That's why "rank" is unambiguous — you can count either way. It's why an $m\times n$ matrix has rank at most $\min(m,n)$: you can't have more independent columns than there are rows to hold them. This fact underlies low-rank approximation and SVD (Session 7). See MML §2.6.

Section 5In NumPy

import numpy as np

# columns as vectors — are they independent?
indep = np.array([[1, 0], [0, 1]], dtype=float)
dep   = np.array([[1, 2], [2, 4]], dtype=float)   # col2 = 2*col1

print(np.linalg.matrix_rank(indep))   # 2  full rank
print(np.linalg.matrix_rank(dep))     # 1  rank-deficient!
print(np.linalg.det(dep))             # 0.0  confirms singular

# a redundant feature makes a feature matrix rank-deficient
price = np.array([100, 200, 300.])
X = np.column_stack([price, price/1000])   # 2nd col carries no new info
print(np.linalg.matrix_rank(X))       # 1, not 2
2 1 0.0 1
rank uses a tolerance np.linalg.matrix_rank treats tiny singular values as zero (numerical tolerance). Real-world features are rarely exactly dependent — they're nearly dependent (highly correlated), which is "practical" rank deficiency and causes the same instability. Watch the condition number, not just exact rank.

Section 6Where it lives in real ML

  • Multicollinearity. If two features are (near-)linearly dependent, the feature matrix is rank-deficient, $X^\top X$ isn't invertible, and linear-regression coefficients become unstable or undefined. Diagnosing this is a standard modelling step.
  • The dummy-variable trap. One-hot encode a category into $k$ columns and keep an intercept, and those columns sum to the all-ones vector — a linear dependence. The fix (drop one column) is literally restoring independence.
  • Effective dimensionality. A dataset with 100 features but rank 12 really "lives" in 12 dimensions; the other 88 are combinations. That gap is what PCA (Session 7) exploits to compress.
  • Low-rank structure. Recommender systems and LoRA assume the important information is low-rank — a few independent directions explain most of the data.
The one-line takeaway Span asks "how much can we reach?"; independence asks "is anyone redundant?"; a basis is the perfect non-redundant spanning set; rank counts the real directions. Redundant features = rank deficiency = trouble — and the cure is finding the true, smaller basis (Session 7).

ReferenceEdge cases, rules & traps

Edge cases & gotchas

  • The zero vector is dependent with anything (0·nothing already gives it) — a set containing $\mathbf{0}$ is never independent.
  • More vectors than dimensions are always dependent: 3 vectors in $\mathbb{R}^2$ can't all be independent (max independent set = dimension).
  • Near-dependence ≠ exact dependence: correlated features are numerically almost dependent — same practical harm, but exact rank may still read "full."
  • Rank ≤ min(rows, cols): a tall skinny $1000\times5$ matrix has rank at most 5.

Golden rules

  1. Span = everything reachable by scaling and adding the vectors.
  2. Independent = no vector is a combination of the others (only the trivial combo makes $\mathbf{0}$).
  3. A basis is independent and spanning; dimension = its size.
  4. Rank = number of independent columns (= rows) = dimension of the span.
  5. Square matrix: full rank ⟺ det≠0 ⟺ invertible ⟺ independent columns.
Trap 1 · Redundant features in regression Duplicated or derived columns (e.g. price and price/1000, or perfectly correlated features) make $X$ rank-deficient and blow up coefficient estimates. Check rank / correlation before fitting.
Trap 2 · The dummy-variable trap One-hot all $k$ categories + an intercept ⇒ linear dependence (columns sum to ones). Drop one category, or drop the intercept.
Trap 3 · Trusting exact rank on real data Floating-point and noise mean features are rarely exactly dependent. Use condition number / near-zero singular values, not just matrix_rank, to catch near-collinearity.

PracticeExercises

Hints only — solve in the paired practice.ipynb, reasoning geometrically first.

E1easy

Are $[1,2]$ and $[2,4]$ independent? Are $[1,2]$ and $[2,1]$? Decide by eye, then check the rank of each pair as columns.

Show solution

First pair: dependent (rank 1, one is 2× the other). Second: independent (rank 2).

np.linalg.matrix_rank([[1,2],[2,4]])   # 1
np.linalg.matrix_rank([[1,2],[2,1]])   # 2
E2easy

Can 3 vectors in $\mathbb{R}^2$ ever be linearly independent? Why or why not?

Show solution

No. The maximum number of independent vectors equals the dimension (2). Any third vector in the plane is already a combination of two independent ones — so at most 2 can be independent.

E3medium

Build a 3×3 matrix whose third column is the sum of the first two. Predict its rank, then verify. What's its determinant?

Show solution

Rank 2 (third column is dependent), so $\det = 0$.

A = np.array([[1,0,1],[0,1,1],[0,0,0.]])  # c3 = c1 + c2
np.linalg.matrix_rank(A), np.linalg.det(A)   # (2, 0.0)
E4medium

Simulate the dummy-variable trap: one-hot encode 3 categories, add a column of ones (intercept), and show the matrix is rank-deficient.

Show solution
oh = np.array([[1,0,0],[0,1,0],[0,0,1],[1,0,0.]])
X = np.column_stack([oh, np.ones(4)])   # 4 cols
np.linalg.matrix_rank(X)                    # 3, not 4 (cols sum to ones)

The three one-hot columns already sum to the intercept column — a dependence. Dropping one category restores full rank.

E5hard

Given $[1,1,0]$ and $[0,1,1]$, find a third vector that (a) keeps the set independent, and (b) one that makes it dependent.

Show solution

(a) e.g. $[0,0,1]$ — points in a new direction, rank becomes 3. (b) e.g. $[1,2,1] = [1,1,0]+[0,1,1]$ — a combination of the first two, so rank stays 2.

np.linalg.matrix_rank(np.array([[1,0,0],[1,1,0],[0,1,1]]).T)  # 3
np.linalg.matrix_rank(np.array([[1,0,1],[1,1,2],[0,1,1]]).T)  # 2

InterviewQuestions you should be able to answer

Q1 · What's the span of a set of vectors?
Every point reachable as a linear combination of them. Two independent vectors in ℝ² span the whole plane; parallel ones span only a line.
Q2 · Define linear independence.
No vector is a combination of the others — equivalently, the only coefficients making them sum to the zero vector are all zero. Dependent means at least one is redundant.
Q3 · What is rank, and how does it relate to invertibility?
The number of independent columns (= rows) = dimension of the column space. A square matrix is invertible iff it's full rank iff det≠0 iff its columns are independent.
Q4 · Why does multicollinearity hurt linear regression?
Correlated/redundant features make X rank-deficient, so XᵀX is (near-)singular and can't be stably inverted — coefficients blow up or aren't unique.
Q5 · What is the dummy-variable trap?
One-hot encoding all k categories plus an intercept creates linear dependence (the dummies sum to the intercept). Drop one category or the intercept to restore full rank.

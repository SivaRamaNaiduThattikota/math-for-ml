SVD, PCA & low-rank

Every matrix is a rotation, a stretch, and a rotation. That one fact powers PCA, compression, recommenders, and LoRA.

~22 min readNumPy paired notebookPrereq: Eigenvalues, Rank

Eigenvectors (Session 6) were beautiful but fussy: they need a square matrix, and even then may be complex or missing. Real data matrices are rectangular — 10,000 rows by 300 columns. The singular value decomposition fixes everything: every matrix, any shape, factors into a rotation, a stretch, and another rotation. From that one fact flow PCA, image compression, recommender systems, and the low-rank adapters (LoRA) used to fine-tune today's large language models.

This is the capstone of the linear-algebra pillar. If you understand "any matrix = rotate → stretch → rotate," you understand the single most useful factorization in machine learning.

By the end you will be able to
  • Read the SVD $A = U\Sigma V^\top$ geometrically: rotate, stretch, rotate — for any matrix.
  • Interpret singular values as sorted stretch factors, and rank as the count of non-zero ones.
  • Build the best rank-$k$ approximation by keeping the top singular values (compression).
  • Explain PCA as the eigenvectors of the covariance matrix / the SVD of centered data.
  • Name where SVD shows up: PCA, recommenders, image compression, and LoRA.

Section 1SVD: every matrix is rotate → stretch → rotate 📖 MML §4.5

The singular value decomposition factors any $m\times n$ matrix into three simple pieces:

$$ A = U\,\Sigma\,V^\top $$

Read it right-to-left as a pipeline (Session 4): $V^\top$ rotates the input into a nice frame, $\Sigma$ stretches each axis by a singular value, and $U$ rotates the result into place. $U$ and $V$ are orthogonal (pure rotations/reflections — they preserve lengths and angles), and $\Sigma$ is diagonal with non-negative entries. The cleanest way to see it: a matrix always maps the unit circle to an ellipse, and the ellipse's axes are the singular values along the directions of $U$.

Your Power BI / SQL anchor Picture a wide table of correlated metrics. SVD finds the few underlying "factors" that explain most of the variation — like discovering that twenty KPIs are really driven by three trends. It's the mathematical engine behind "reduce these columns to the handful that matter," which is exactly what PCA does below.

Section 2Singular values — sorted stretch factors

The singular values $\sigma_1 \ge \sigma_2 \ge \dots \ge 0$ are the ellipse's axis lengths, always non-negative and sorted largest-first. They carry a lot of information at a glance:

  • The number of non-zero $\sigma_i$ is the rank (Session 5). A zero singular value = a direction squashed flat.
  • $\sigma_1$ is the maximum stretch the matrix applies to any unit vector; $\sigma_n$ the minimum.
  • The condition number $\sigma_1/\sigma_n$ measures how "close to singular" a matrix is — huge values mean numerically unstable inverses (the practical near-collinearity of Session 5).
Link back to eigenvalues (Session 6) Singular values are the square roots of the eigenvalues of $A^\top A$: $\sigma_i = \sqrt{\lambda_i(A^\top A)}$. And for a symmetric positive-semidefinite matrix, the SVD and the eigendecomposition coincide. SVD is the honest generalization of eigenvectors to matrices that aren't square or symmetric.

Section 3Low-rank approximation — compression that keeps what matters

The SVD lets you rebuild $A$ as a sum of rank-1 pieces, each weighted by a singular value:

$$ A = \sigma_1 \mathbf{u}_1\mathbf{v}_1^\top + \sigma_2\mathbf{u}_2\mathbf{v}_2^\top + \dots $$

Because the pieces are sorted by importance, keeping only the top $k$ gives $A_k$ — and a famous result (the Eckart–Young theorem) says this is the best possible rank-$k$ approximation of $A$. Truncating the tiny singular values throws away noise/detail while preserving structure.

$$ A \approx A_k = \sum_{i=1}^{k}\sigma_i\mathbf{u}_i\mathbf{v}_i^\top $$

The compression is real: a $1000\times1000$ image is a million numbers, but a rank-50 approximation stores only $50\times(1000+1000+1) \approx 100{,}000$ — a 10× saving, often visually indistinguishable. This "the important information is low-rank" assumption is everywhere in ML.

Section 4PCA — the max-variance directions of your data 📖 MML Ch 10

Principal Component Analysis is the flagship application. Given data with correlated features, PCA finds a new set of orthogonal axes — the principal components — ordered so the first captures the most variance, the second the next most, and so on. Project onto the top few and you've reduced dimensionality while keeping most of the signal.

The recipe is pure Sessions 6–7:

  1. Center the data (subtract the mean of each feature).
  2. Form the covariance matrix $C = \frac{1}{n-1}X^\top X$ — symmetric, so (spectral theorem, Session 6) it has real eigenvalues and orthogonal eigenvectors.
  3. The eigenvectors of $C$ are the principal components; each eigenvalue is the variance captured along that component.
  4. Keep the top-$k$ components → project → done.
Going deeper — PCA is just the SVD of the centered data

You don't even need to form the covariance matrix. If $X$ is your centered data ($n$ rows, $d$ features) with SVD $X = U\Sigma V^\top$, then:

$$ X^\top X = V\Sigma^\top U^\top U \Sigma V^\top = V\,\Sigma^2\,V^\top $$

That's an eigendecomposition of (a scalar multiple of) the covariance: the right singular vectors $V$ are the principal components, and the squared singular values are proportional to the variances. So "PCA" and "SVD of centered data" are the same computation — which is exactly how libraries implement it (more stably than eigendecomposing $X^\top X$). See MML §4.5 & Ch 10.

Section 5In NumPy ✓ outputs verified

import numpy as np

A = np.array([[3, 1], [1, 2], [0, 1]], dtype=float)   # 3x2, rectangular!
U, S, Vt = np.linalg.svd(A, full_matrices=False)
print(S)                              # singular values, sorted (largest first)

# rank-1 approximation: keep only the top singular value
A1 = S[0] * np.outer(U[:, 0], Vt[0])
print(np.linalg.matrix_rank(A1))     # 1

# PCA via SVD of centered data
X = np.random.rand(100, 5)
Xc = X - X.mean(axis=0)              # center
U, S, Vt = np.linalg.svd(Xc, full_matrices=False)
PCs = Vt                              # rows are principal components
var = S**2 / (len(X) - 1)          # variance per component
print((var / var.sum()).round(3))    # explained-variance ratio
[3.65861951 1.61700716] 1 [0.27 0.23 0.19 0.17 0.14] # (example) how much variance each PC explains
Center first; use full_matrices=False PCA on un-centered data finds directions dominated by the mean, not the variance — always subtract the mean first. And full_matrices=False gives the compact, economical SVD (skips the parts you never use), which is what you want for tall data matrices.

Section 6Where it lives in real ML

  • PCA — dimensionality reduction for visualization (project to 2-D), denoising, decorrelation, and speeding up downstream models.
  • Recommender systems — matrix factorization of the user×item ratings matrix is a low-rank SVD: the singular vectors are latent "taste" factors.
  • Image & data compression — truncated SVD (Eckart–Young) stores the top components only.
  • LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation) — fine-tuning an LLM by learning a low-rank update $\Delta W = BA$ instead of all the weights. It works because the useful adaptation is low-rank — the exact premise of this session.
  • Latent Semantic Analysis — SVD of a term×document matrix uncovers topics; the pseudo-inverse (least squares) is built from the SVD too.
The one-line takeaway SVD says every matrix is rotate → stretch → rotate, and the stretches (singular values) rank the directions by importance. Keep the top few → PCA, compression, recommenders, LoRA. This is where linear algebra pays for the whole course.

ReferenceEdge cases, rules & traps

Edge cases & gotchas

  • SVD always exists — for any real matrix, any shape. Eigendecomposition doesn't.
  • Singular values are unique (and $\ge 0$); the singular vectors can flip sign or rotate within a repeated-$\sigma$ subspace.
  • Center before PCA, and usually standardize (scale features) too — otherwise a large-unit feature hijacks the components (Session 1's feature scaling returns).
  • Explained-variance ratio ($\sigma_i^2/\sum\sigma_j^2$) tells you how many components to keep — look for the "elbow."

Golden rules

  1. $A=U\Sigma V^\top$ for any matrix: rotate → stretch → rotate.
  2. Singular values = sorted stretch factors; count of non-zero ones = rank.
  3. Top-$k$ singular pieces = best rank-$k$ approximation (Eckart–Young).
  4. PCA = eigenvectors of the covariance = right singular vectors of centered data; eigenvalues/σ² = variance.
  5. Center (and usually scale) your data before PCA.
Trap 1 · Forgetting to center (or scale) Un-centered PCA points at the mean; un-scaled PCA points at whatever feature has the biggest units. Standardize first, or your "principal components" are artifacts.
Trap 2 · Reading singular-vector signs as meaningful Signs (and directions within tied singular values) are arbitrary and vary by library/run. Don't interpret a PC's sign; interpret the direction/axis.
Trap 3 · Eigendecomposing XᵀX directly Forming $X^\top X$ squares the condition number and loses precision. Prefer SVD of $X$ itself — it's what PCA implementations use.

Compute the SVD of $\begin{bmatrix}3&0\\0&2\end{bmatrix}$ by inspection. What are its singular values and singular vectors?

E2easy

Verify $\sigma_i=\sqrt{\lambda_i(A^\top A)}$ for a random 3×2 matrix.

Show solution
A = np.random.rand(3,2)
S = np.linalg.svd(A, compute_uv=False)
lam = np.sort(np.linalg.eigvals(A.T @ A).real)[::-1]
np.allclose(S, np.sqrt(lam))    # True
E3medium

Reconstruct a matrix from its full SVD, then from only its top singular value; compare.

Show solution
A = np.random.rand(4,4)
U,S,Vt = np.linalg.svd(A)
full = U @ np.diag(S) @ Vt                 # == A
A1   = S[0] * np.outer(U[:,0], Vt[0])       # rank-1 approx
np.linalg.norm(A-full), np.linalg.norm(A-A1)  # ~0 , larger
E4medium

Generate 2-D data stretched along the line $y=x$, run PCA via SVD, and confirm the first PC points along $[1,1]$.

Show solution
t = np.random.randn(500)
X = np.column_stack([t, t + 0.1*np.random.randn(500)])
Xc = X - X.mean(0)
_,S,Vt = np.linalg.svd(Xc, full_matrices=False)
Vt[0]    # ~[0.707, 0.707] (or its negative) -> along y=x
E5hard

Explained variance: on 5-D random data, compute the ratio $\sigma_i^2/\sum\sigma_j^2$ and the cumulative sum. How many components to keep for 90%?

Show solution
X = np.random.rand(200,5); Xc = X - X.mean(0)
S = np.linalg.svd(Xc, compute_uv=False)
ratio = S**2/np.sum(S**2)
np.cumsum(ratio)     # find the first index >= 0.90

Count the components until the cumulative ratio crosses 0.90 — that's your $k$. The "elbow" in this curve is the standard way to choose it.

InterviewQuestions you should be able to answer

Q1 · What is the SVD, geometrically?
$A=U\Sigma V^\top$: any linear map is a rotation ($V^\top$), an axis-wise stretch by the singular values ($\Sigma$), then another rotation ($U$). It maps the unit sphere to an ellipsoid whose axes are the singular values.
Q2 · How does SVD relate to eigendecomposition?
Singular values are √eigenvalues of $A^\top A$; the right singular vectors are its eigenvectors. For symmetric PSD matrices, SVD = eigendecomposition. SVD generalizes it to any shape.
Q3 · How is PCA computed, and what do the components mean?
Center the data, take the SVD (or eigendecompose the covariance); the right singular vectors / covariance eigenvectors are the principal components, and σ²/eigenvalues give the variance each captures. Keep the top-k.
Q4 · What does low-rank approximation give you, and why is it optimal?
Keeping the top-k singular components is the best rank-k approximation in Frobenius/spectral norm (Eckart–Young) — maximal information for the storage, used for compression and denoising.
Q5 · Where does SVD show up in modern ML?
PCA, recommender matrix factorization, image compression, LSA, the pseudo-inverse for least squares, and LoRA (low-rank weight updates for LLM fine-tuning).

Vectors

A row in your table is a point in space. Once you truly believe that sentence, the rest of machine learning is geometry.

~18 min readNumPy paired notebookPrereq: none

Every machine-learning model you have ever run — the fraud classifier, the churn model, the retrieval engine in OpsRAG — begins by throwing away the human-readable version of your data and replacing each record with a list of numbers. That list is a vector. Nothing else in this course makes sense until vectors feel like second nature, because a vector is the atom every other idea is built from: matrices are stacks of them, gradients are them, embeddings are them, model weights are them.

This session has one job: to make you fluent at moving between the three ways of seeing a vector, and comfortable with the handful of operations that everything downstream reuses.

By the end you will be able to
  • See any data row as a point in $\mathbb{R}^n$ — and know why that lets ML treat tables as geometry.
  • Add, scale, and subtract vectors, and read each operation both algebraically and geometrically.
  • Compute L2 / L1 norms and explain how they become Ridge and Lasso regularization.
  • Turn "similar" into "close" via distance = ‖a − b‖, and explain why feature scaling comes first.
  • Write all of it as vectorized NumPy — no loops — and dodge the broadcasting / zero-vector traps.

Section 1Three ways to see the same object

A vector is, most literally, an ordered list of numbers:

$$ \mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix} \qquad\text{written inline as}\qquad \mathbf{v} = [3,\ 4] $$

"Ordered" is doing real work in that sentence: [3, 4] and [4, 3] are different vectors, because position carries meaning — the first slot might be tenure and the second monthly charge. Swapping them scrambles the data. Now the same object, seen three ways:

DefinitionScalar — a single number, no direction (e.g. 7). Vector — an ordered tuple of scalars. The scalars are its components.
  • As an arrow. Start at the origin, walk 3 right and 4 up. The vector is that arrow — it has a length and a direction.
  • As a point. Forget the arrow, keep the tip: the location (3, 4). This is the view ML uses most — every row is a dot floating in space.
  • As data. One customer: [tenure = 3, charge = 4]. The whole dataset is a cloud of such points, and "learning" means finding structure in that cloud.
Your Power BI / SQL anchor One row of a table, keeping only its numeric columns, is a vector. A table with 10 numeric columns is a cloud of points in 10-dimensional space. You already built these objects every day in Power BI — you just called them rows. The mental upgrade is small but total: a row is not a record in a grid, it is a location.

Dimensions are just "how many numbers"

A vector with $n$ components lives in $\mathbb{R}^n$ — read aloud as "R-n," the space of all real vectors with $n$ slots. This notation looks intimidating and means almost nothing:

  • A 2-D point is in $\mathbb{R}^2$ — you can draw it.
  • A flattened $28\times28$ grayscale image is in $\mathbb{R}^{784}$ — you cannot draw it, but the algebra is identical.
  • A sentence embedding from OpsRAG's all-MiniLM-L6-v2 is in $\mathbb{R}^{384}$.

The crucial and slightly magical fact: every operation in this lesson works the same in 2 dimensions and in 784. We develop intuition where we can see (2-D), then apply it blindly and correctly in dimensions we can't. That transfer is the entire reason a spreadsheet can be treated as geometry.

Section 2What the components actually mean

It is tempting to treat [3, 4] as an opaque pair. It is worth spending thirty seconds on what it decomposes into, because that decomposition is the seed of basis vectors, PCA, and embeddings later on. The vector [3, 4] is shorthand for:

$$ [3,4] \;=\; 3\underbrace{[1,0]}_{\hat{x}} \;+\; 4\underbrace{[0,1]}_{\hat{y}} $$
Thread forwardRight now the axes are the obvious x̂ and ŷ. In PCA we'll choose new axes aligned with the data's spread — same vector, smarter coordinates. Hold onto this picture. ↳ Session 7

In words: "three steps along the x-axis, four along the y-axis." The numbers are coordinates relative to a chosen set of axes (here the standard axes $\hat{x}=[1,0]$ and $\hat{y}=[0,1]$). Nothing forces us to use those axes — and the day we pick better ones is the day PCA happens. For now, just register that a vector's components are always relative to some frame of reference.

Section 3The operations that everything reuses

Addition — walk one arrow, then the other

Vector addition is component-wise:

$$ [4,2] + [1,3] = [4{+}1,\; 2{+}3] = [5,5] $$

Geometrically there are two equivalent pictures, and you want both in your head. Tip-to-tail: place the tail of b at the tip of a; the sum runs from the origin to where you end up. Parallelogram: the sum is the diagonal of the parallelogram spanned by a and b (that is the dashed shape in Figure 1). Because addition is component-wise, it is commutative — $\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{b}=\mathbf{b}+\mathbf{a}$ — which is why the parallelogram is symmetric.

Where does this show up in ML? Any time contributions combine linearly: a linear model's prediction is a sum of weighted feature vectors; a residual is actual − predicted, a vector subtraction; a gradient-descent step is "current weights + a small correction vector." Addition is not a toy — it is the update rule of learning.

Scalar multiplication — stretch, don't turn

$$ 2\cdot[4,2] = [8,4] \qquad -1\cdot[4,2] = [-4,-2] \qquad 0\cdot[4,2] = [0,0] $$

Multiplying a vector by a scalar scales its length and leaves its direction untouched — except a negative scalar flips it 180°, and zero collapses it to the origin. This is exactly the learning rate in gradient descent: you compute a direction to move (a vector), then multiply it by a small scalar to take a cautious step.

Subtraction — the difference vector points from b to a

$\mathbf{a}-\mathbf{b}$ is just $\mathbf{a}+(-\mathbf{b})$, so it is still component-wise. But its geometry is the single most useful picture in this lesson:

$$ \mathbf{a}-\mathbf{b} \;\text{ is the arrow that points from the tip of } \mathbf{b} \text{ to the tip of } \mathbf{a}. $$
Why this mattersThe difference vector is how ML measures error (prediction − target) and separation (point − point). Its length is a distance; its direction is where gradients point. Two of the biggest ideas in the field are hiding in one subtraction. ↳ Session 11

That is why the distance between two data points is the length of their difference vector — a fact we cash in two sections from now for k-nearest-neighbours and clustering.

Section 4How big is a vector? Norms 📖 MML §3.1

The magnitude (or norm) of a vector is its length. The default — the one people mean when they just say "length" or write $\lVert\mathbf{v}\rVert$ — is the $L_2$ norm, which is Pythagoras extended to any number of dimensions:

$$ \lVert \mathbf{v} \rVert_2 = \sqrt{v_1^2 + v_2^2 + \dots + v_n^2} \qquad \lVert[4,2]\rVert_2 = \sqrt{16+4} = \sqrt{20} \approx 4.47 $$

But $L_2$ is not the only way to measure size, and the alternatives are not academic — they show up as the two most common forms of regularization:

Thread forwardWhen you add a norm of the weight vector to a loss, you get regularization: L2 → Ridge (shrinks weights smoothly), L1 → Lasso (drives some weights to exactly 0 → feature selection). Same norms, different geometry, different behaviour. Returns in the ML-theory track.
  • $L_2$ (Euclidean): $\sqrt{\sum v_i^2}$. Straight-line distance. Behind Ridge regression.
  • $L_1$ (Manhattan / taxicab): $\sum |v_i|$. Distance if you can only move along axes. Behind Lasso, and it encourages sparsity.
  • $L_\infty$ (max): $\max_i |v_i|$. The single largest component. Used in some robustness bounds.
Going deeper — what makes something a "norm"?

We've been calling these "ways to measure size," but a norm has a precise definition. A function $\lVert\cdot\rVert:\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}$ is a norm if, for all vectors $\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}$ and scalars $\lambda$, it satisfies three axioms:

$$ \begin{aligned} &\text{1. Positive definite:} && \lVert\mathbf{x}\rVert \ge 0, \text{ and } =0 \iff \mathbf{x}=\mathbf{0} \\ &\text{2. Absolutely homogeneous:} && \lVert\lambda\mathbf{x}\rVert = |\lambda|\,\lVert\mathbf{x}\rVert \\ &\text{3. Triangle inequality:} && \lVert\mathbf{x}+\mathbf{y}\rVert \le \lVert\mathbf{x}\rVert + \lVert\mathbf{y}\rVert \end{aligned} $$

$L_2$, $L_1$, and $L_\infty$ all satisfy these — which is why they're interchangeable notions of length, each valid, just measuring differently. Axiom 2 is exactly why scalar multiplication scales length (Section 3), and axiom 3 says "a detour is never shorter than the direct path." Formal treatment: MML §3.1.

Unit vectors: length without magnitude

Dividing a vector by its own norm gives a unit vector — same direction, length exactly 1:

$$ \hat{\mathbf{v}} = \frac{\mathbf{v}}{\lVert\mathbf{v}\rVert} $$

You normalize whenever you care about direction and not size — the canonical case being text embeddings, where two documents are "similar" if their vectors point the same way, regardless of length. There is exactly one landmine here: the zero vector has no direction, so normalizing it divides by zero and yields nan. Guard for it every single time.

Section 5How far apart are two vectors? 📖 MML §3.3

Combine the last two ideas — subtraction gives a difference vector, the norm gives its length — and you get distance:

$$ \text{dist}(\mathbf{a},\mathbf{b}) = \lVert \mathbf{a}-\mathbf{b} \rVert = \sqrt{\sum_i (a_i-b_i)^2} $$

This one formula is the beating heart of a surprising amount of ML: k-nearest-neighbours classifies a point by the labels of the closest points; k-means clusters by assigning each point to its nearest centre; retrieval systems (including a naive version of OpsRAG) rank documents by distance to the query vector. "Similar" almost always cashes out as "close," and "close" almost always cashes out as the norm of a difference.

Section 6Doing it in NumPy — and why NumPy ✓ outputs verified

Everything above is one line each in NumPy, and the fact that it is one line — not a loop — is itself a lesson.

import numpy as np

a = np.array([4, 2], dtype=float)
b = np.array([1, 3], dtype=float)

print(a + b)                 # [5. 5.]  component-wise add
print(2 * a)                 # [8. 4.]  scalar multiply
print(np.linalg.norm(a))      # 4.4721   L2 magnitude
print(np.linalg.norm(a, 1))   # 6.0      L1 (Manhattan)
print(a / np.linalg.norm(a))   # unit vector, length 1
print(np.linalg.norm(a - b))  # 3.1623   distance between points
[5. 5.] [8. 4.] 4.47213595499958 6.0 [0.89442719 0.4472136 ] 3.1622776601683795
Vectorization — verified Notice there is no for loop anywhere. NumPy applies each operation to the whole array at once, in compiled C, over contiguous memory. On a million-element vector this is 10–100× faster than a Python loop and reads far cleaner. "Vectorize your code" — the advice you will hear constantly — literally means "express it as whole-array operations like these instead of element-by-element loops."
Broadcasting — convenient, and a trap a + 10 does not error — NumPy broadcasts the scalar and adds 10 to every component. Powerful, but it also means a genuine shape mistake can silently produce a plausible-looking wrong answer. When a result looks off, print .shape before anything else.

Section 7Feature scaling, in one worked example

Here is the most important practical consequence of everything above, and a favourite interview probe. Two houses, each described by [square_feet, bedrooms]:

$$ \mathbf{h_1}=[1200,\,3], \quad \mathbf{h_2}=[1400,\,2], \quad \text{dist} = \sqrt{200^2 + 1^2} = \sqrt{40001} \approx 200.0 $$

The distance is 200.0 — and the bedroom difference contributed a laughable 1 out of that. Square footage, measured in the thousands, completely dominates; bedrooms may as well not exist. A distance-based model would treat two houses as "similar" based on square footage alone. Now rescale square footage into thousands:

$$ \mathbf{h_1}=[1.2,\,3], \quad \mathbf{h_2}=[1.4,\,2], \quad \text{dist} = \sqrt{0.2^2 + 1^2} = \sqrt{1.04} \approx 1.02 $$

Now bedrooms dominate. Neither raw scale is "correct" — which is the point. Feature scaling (standardization or min-max normalization) puts every feature on comparable footing before any distance is computed, so the model weighs them by importance rather than by accident of units. This is why StandardScaler sits at the front of nearly every scikit-learn pipeline you'll write — including the ones already in your fraud and churn projects.

Section 8High dimensions & the intuition tax

Two honest warnings about $\mathbb{R}^n$ for large $n$, so the geometry doesn't mislead you:

  • You lose the picture, keep the algebra. Every formula here holds in 784-D; your visualization of it does not. Trust the algebra, distrust the mental cartoon.
  • The curse of dimensionality. As $n$ grows, points spread out until everything is roughly equidistant from everything — distances lose contrast, and "nearest neighbour" becomes barely meaningful. This is precisely why dimensionality reduction (PCA, Session 7) and learned low-dimensional embeddings exist: fewer, better dimensions beat many raw ones.

ReferenceEdge cases, rules & traps

Edge cases & gotchas

  • Zero vector $[0,\dots,0]$ has magnitude 0 and no direction — normalizing it is undefined (nan).
  • Dimension mismatch: you cannot add $[1,2]$ to $[1,2,3]$. Shapes must match (or broadcast deliberately).
  • Row vs column: math writes column vectors; a NumPy 1-D array has shape (n,) — neither row nor column until reshaped. This will bite in matrix multiply (Session 4).
  • Integer dtype: np.array([1,2]); a /= 3 truncates on an int array. Use float for ML.
Trap 1 · Silent broadcastingvec + 10 or a (3,) plus a (3,1) won't error — they produce a shape you didn't intend. Print .shape first when debugging.
Trap 2 · Normalizing the zero vectorAlways check norm > 0 before dividing, or you'll silently inject nans that poison every downstream computation.
Trap 3 · Comparing unscaled featuresAny distance, KNN, or K-means on raw features is dominated by whichever feature has the largest units. Scale first — always.

Golden rules

  1. A vector carries magnitude and direction — almost every question is really about one or the other.
  2. Add and subtract are component-wise; the shapes must match (or broadcast deliberately).
  3. Scalar multiplication changes length, not direction (a negative flips it, zero collapses it).
  4. Normalize when only direction matters — and guard the zero vector first.
  5. Distance = ‖a − b‖ — the norm of the difference vector.
  6. Prefer one vectorized NumPy operation over any element-wise loop.

Compute ‖[6,8]‖₂ by hand.It's a clean integer — a Pythagorean triple.

Show solution

$\sqrt{6^2+8^2}=\sqrt{36+64}=\sqrt{100}=\mathbf{10}$. The 6-8-10 triple (a scaled 3-4-5).

np.linalg.norm([6,8])   # 10.0
E3medium

Write unit(v) that normalizes a vector but returns the zero vector unchanged instead of nan.Check the norm before dividing.

Show solution
def unit(v):
    v = np.asarray(v, dtype=float)
    n = np.linalg.norm(v)
    return v if n == 0 else v / n

unit([3,4])     # [0.6 0.8]  (length 1)
unit([0,0])     # [0. 0.]    (no nan)

The guard n == 0 is the whole point — without it you divide by zero and poison everything downstream.

E4medium

Reproduce Section 7: compute the house distance before and after scaling sqft to thousands. Explain in one sentence why the "closest" house flips.

Show solution
h1 = np.array([1200,3]); h2 = np.array([1400,2])
np.linalg.norm(h1-h2)                    # 200.0025  -> sqft dominates

s1 = np.array([1.2,3]);  s2 = np.array([1.4,2])
np.linalg.norm(s1-s2)                    # 1.0198   -> bedrooms dominate

Why it flips: Euclidean distance is driven by whichever feature has the largest raw spread, so rescaling changes which feature "wins" and therefore which points count as nearest.

E5hard

Without np.linalg.norm, compute the magnitude of a length-1000 random vector using only np.dot.‖v‖ = √(v · v). This identity is the doorway to Session 2.

Show solution
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)
v = rng.standard_normal(1000)
mag = np.sqrt(np.dot(v, v))
mag, np.allclose(mag, np.linalg.norm(v))   # (~31.6, True)

A vector dotted with itself is the sum of its squared components — i.e. $\lVert v\rVert^2$. Take the square root and you have the norm. Session 2 generalizes v·w to any pair.

PayoffWhere this lives in real ML

  • Features are vectors. Every row in your fraud/churn projects was a point in $\mathbb{R}^n$; the model learned a decision surface over that space.
  • Embeddings are vectors. OpsRAG maps each answer to a ~384-D vector; "similar meaning" = "nearby direction" (Session 2's dot product).
  • Images are vectors. Your defect-detection images are long vectors before a CNN gives them structure.
  • Weights are vectors, gradients are vectors. Training = repeatedly nudging a weight vector along the negative gradient (Sessions 8–9).

InterviewQuestions you should be able to answer

Q1 · Magnitude vs direction — when do you care about each?
Direction for semantic similarity (embeddings → cosine); magnitude when scale is meaningful (raw distance, gradient step size, regularization penalty).
Q2 · Why scale features before a distance-based model?
Large-unit features dominate Euclidean distance regardless of importance, so the model effectively ignores small-unit features. Scaling equalizes their influence. (See Section 7.)
Q3 · Difference between L1 and L2 norms, and why an ML engineer cares?
L2 = √Σvᵢ² (straight line), L1 = Σ|vᵢ| (taxicab). As regularizers: L2→Ridge (smooth shrinkage), L1→Lasso (sparsity / feature selection).
Q4 · A 28×28 image as a vector — dimension, and the consequence?
784-D. High dimensionality triggers the curse of dimensionality (distances lose contrast), motivating PCA and learned embeddings.
Q5 · What does normalizing do, and when is it undefined?
Scales to length 1, preserving direction (projects onto the unit sphere). Undefined for the zero vector.

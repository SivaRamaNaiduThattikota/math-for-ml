Bayesian inference & conjugate priors

Update beliefs with data: prior → posterior. The Beta-Binomial conjugate pair, plus the distribution zoo (Poisson, Exponential, Beta, Gamma).

~20 min readNumPy + SciPy notebookPrereq: Bayes, Distributions

Session 16 was frequentist: fixed-but-unknown parameters, p-values, confidence intervals. There's another way to reason, and it's the one that feels most like learning. In Bayesian inference you hold a distribution of belief over a parameter, and each new data point updates it — prior → posterior — exactly the Bayes rule from Session 12, now run continuously. With the right prior (a conjugate one), that update is a one-line arithmetic shortcut. This is the machinery behind Bayesian A/B tests, bandits, and the priors that regularize models.

By the end you will be able to
  • Update a belief with data: prior → posterior, and read a credible interval.
  • Explain conjugate priors and why they make updating trivial.
  • Use the Beta-Binomial pair — the canonical conjugate example.
  • Place the distribution zoo — Poisson, Exponential, Beta, Gamma — and what each models.
  • Connect Bayesian thinking to regularization, bandits, and uncertainty.

Section 1Bayesian updating — belief as a distribution

Bayes' theorem (Session 12), read as a learning rule:

$$ \underbrace{P(\theta\mid D)}_{\text{posterior}} \;\propto\; \underbrace{P(D\mid\theta)}_{\text{likelihood}}\;\underbrace{P(\theta)}_{\text{prior}} $$

Instead of estimating a single number, you carry a whole distribution over the parameter and sharpen it as evidence arrives: today's posterior becomes tomorrow's prior. From that posterior you can read a credible interval — "there's a 95% probability the parameter lies in [a, b]." Note that's a genuinely different (and more intuitive) statement than a frequentist confidence interval (Session 16), which was about the procedure's long-run coverage, not this one interval.

Your Power BI / SQL anchor It's how you actually update a forecast: you start with a belief ("conversion is probably around 10%"), watch a few days of data, and revise — not to one new number, but to a sharper range of plausible values. Bayesian inference formalizes that "revise as data comes in" instinct.

Section 2Conjugate priors — the arithmetic shortcut 📖 MML §6.6

Computing a posterior usually means a messy integral for the normalizer $P(D)$. But for special prior + likelihood pairings the posterior comes out in the same family as the prior — you just update its parameters. These are conjugate priors, and they turn Bayesian updating into simple bookkeeping. The classic examples:

  • Beta prior + Binomial/Bernoulli likelihood → Beta posterior (probabilities).
  • Gamma prior + Poisson likelihood → Gamma posterior (rates/counts).
  • Gaussian prior + Gaussian likelihood → Gaussian posterior (means).

Section 3Beta-Binomial — conjugacy you can watch 📖 MML §6.6

The Beta$(\alpha,\beta)$ distribution lives on $[0,1]$ — perfect as a belief about a probability (a conversion rate, a coin's bias). Its magic: after observing $h$ heads and $t$ tails, the posterior is simply

$$ \text{Beta}(\alpha,\beta) \;\xrightarrow{\ h\text{ heads},\ t\text{ tails}\ }\; \text{Beta}(\alpha+h,\ \beta+t) $$

Add successes to $\alpha$, failures to $\beta$. That's the entire update. The parameters even have a reading: the prior acts like $\alpha-1$ imagined prior heads and $\beta-1$ prior tails ("pseudo-counts"). Add data below and watch the belief sharpen.

MLE vs MAP vs posterior mean For 14 heads in 20 with a Beta(2,2) prior: the MLE is 14/20 = 0.70 (data only); the posterior mean is 16/24 ≈ 0.667; the MAP (posterior mode) ≈ 0.682. The prior gently pulls the estimate toward 0.5 — and that pull is regularization (Session 17). With more data, all three converge.

Section 4The distribution zoo 📖 MML §6.2

Session 13 focused on the Gaussian; here are the other workhorses, each modelling a different kind of quantity:

  • Bernoulli / Binomial — yes/no outcomes and their counts (Session 13).
  • Poisson$(\lambda)$ — counts of rare events in a fixed interval (arrivals, defects, clicks/hour). Mean = variance = $\lambda$.
  • Exponential — waiting time until the next event (time between arrivals); memoryless.
  • Gamma — positive continuous quantities (total waiting time, rates); generalizes the exponential and is conjugate to the Poisson.
  • Beta — a distribution over a probability $\in[0,1]$; the natural prior for rates (above).

Knowing which distribution fits your quantity is half of modelling it — and it tells you which likelihood (hence which loss, Session 17) to use.

Section 5In NumPy / SciPy ✓ outputs verified

import numpy as np
from scipy import stats

# Beta-Binomial: prior Beta(2,2), observe 14 heads / 6 tails
a0, b0 = 2, 2
h, t = 14, 6
a, b = a0 + h, b0 + t                     # posterior params — the whole update
print(a, b, round(a/(a+b), 3))            # 16 8 0.667  (posterior mean)

# 95% credible interval from the posterior
print(np.round(stats.beta.ppf([0.025, 0.975], a, b), 3))   # [0.471 0.836]

# MLE (data only) vs MAP (posterior mode) vs posterior mean
print(h/(h+t), (a-1)/(a+b-2), a/(a+b))    # 0.70  0.682  0.667

# the zoo, sanity-checked
print(stats.poisson.mean(3), stats.poisson.var(3))    # 3.0 3.0  (mean = var)
print(stats.expon.mean(scale=2), stats.gamma.mean(3, scale=2))  # 2.0 6.0

Credible interval ≠ confidence interval A 95% credible interval genuinely means "95% posterior probability the parameter is in here" — a statement about the parameter, given your prior. A frequentist confidence interval (Session 16) is a statement about the procedure across many repeats. They often look similar with lots of data and a flat prior, but they answer different questions.

Section 6Where it lives in real ML

  • Priors are regularization. The MAP estimate (Session 17) is Bayesian: a Gaussian prior = L2, a Laplace prior = L1. Regularization strength is prior strength.
  • Bayesian A/B testing & bandits. Track a Beta posterior per variant; Thompson sampling picks the variant to show by sampling from these posteriors — exploration/exploitation without fixed sample sizes.
  • Uncertainty quantification. Bayesian neural nets, Gaussian processes, and conformal methods give calibrated uncertainty, not just point predictions — vital for high-stakes decisions.
  • Small-data regimes. A sensible prior stabilizes estimates when data is scarce (the pseudo-count effect), where pure MLE overfits.
The one-line takeaway Bayesian inference carries a distribution of belief and updates it with data (posterior ∝ likelihood × prior). Conjugate priors (Beta-Binomial, Gamma-Poisson, Gaussian-Gaussian) make that update pure arithmetic — and the prior is exactly the regularization you already use.
Computational & memory cost With a conjugate prior the posterior update is $O(1)$ — just add to the counts (Beta–Binomial, Gamma–Poisson). Step outside conjugacy and there is no closed form, so you pay the full cost of MCMC (many samples) or variational inference (an optimization) instead.

ReferenceEdge cases, rules & traps

Edge cases & gotchas

  • Priors matter most with little data — and wash out as data grows. State your prior; a strong wrong prior biases small-sample results.
  • Credible ≠ confidence — don't claim frequentist coverage for a Bayesian interval or vice-versa.
  • Conjugacy is a convenience, not a requirement — most real models aren't conjugate and need MCMC / variational inference.
  • Beta(1,1) is uniform — a flat prior; then the posterior mean ≈ MLE (with a +1/+1 smoothing).

Golden rules

  1. Posterior ∝ likelihood × prior; the posterior is your updated belief distribution.
  2. Conjugate prior ⇒ posterior in the same family ⇒ update = adjust parameters.
  3. Beta-Binomial: Beta$(\alpha,\beta)$ + $h$ heads, $t$ tails → Beta$(\alpha+h,\beta+t)$.
  4. Read a credible interval from the posterior; it's a probability statement about the parameter.
  5. The prior is regularization — MAP = MLE + a prior (Session 17).
Trap 1 · Confusing credible and confidence intervals They answer different questions. Credible: "95% posterior probability θ is here." Confidence: "95% of such intervals cover θ across repeats." Say which you mean.
Trap 2 · Over-trusting the prior on small data With few observations the prior dominates the posterior. A biased or over-confident prior skews conclusions — choose weakly-informative priors unless you have real prior knowledge.
Trap 3 · Assuming everything is conjugate Conjugacy is the exception. Real Bayesian models usually need MCMC or variational approximations; don't force a conjugate shortcut where it doesn't hold.

E3medium

Compare MLE, MAP, and posterior mean for the above. Why do they differ, and what makes them converge?

Show solution

MLE 0.70 (data only), MAP 0.682, mean 0.667 — the Beta(2,2) prior pulls toward 0.5. More data shrinks the prior's influence, so all three converge to the true rate.

E4medium

Show the prior washes out: with a Beta(2,2) prior, compare the posterior mean after 4 flips (3H1T) vs after 400 flips (300H100T).

Show solution

4 flips: Beta(5,3), mean 0.625 (pulled from data's 0.75 toward 0.5). 400 flips: Beta(302,102), mean ≈ 0.748 ≈ the data rate 0.75 — the prior is now negligible.

E5hard

Sketch Thompson sampling: two variants with Beta posteriors; sample one draw from each and pick the larger. Simulate which variant gets chosen more when one truly converts better.

Show solution
A=stats.beta(30,70); B=stats.beta(45,55)   # B better (~0.45 vs 0.30)
picks=['B' if B.rvs()>A.rvs() else 'A' for _ in range(10000)]
picks.count('B')/10000   # most picks go to B, but A still explored

Thompson sampling exploits the better variant while still exploring the other in proportion to its posterior probability of being best.

InterviewQuestions you should be able to answer

Q1 · What is Bayesian updating?
Maintaining a distribution of belief over a parameter and revising it with data via posterior ∝ likelihood × prior; the posterior becomes the next prior as more data arrives.
Q2 · What is a conjugate prior, with an example?
A prior whose posterior stays in the same family, so updating is just parameter arithmetic. Beta prior + Binomial likelihood → Beta posterior: Beta(α,β) + h heads, t tails → Beta(α+h, β+t).
Q3 · Credible interval vs confidence interval?
A credible interval is a direct posterior-probability statement about the parameter ("95% probability θ is here"); a confidence interval is about the long-run coverage of the procedure. Different questions.
Q4 · How does Bayesian inference relate to regularization?
MAP estimation maximizes likelihood × prior; the prior's negative log is a penalty — a Gaussian prior gives L2, a Laplace prior gives L1. Regularization is a prior belief.
Q5 · What is Thompson sampling?
A bandit strategy: keep a posterior (e.g. Beta) per action, sample once from each, and take the action with the highest sample. It balances exploration and exploitation via the posteriors' uncertainty.

◆ Concept Card
Priors + data = posterior; conjugacy makes it easy.

Reusable idea: Bayesian inference updates a full distribution over parameters, not just a point.

  • Conjugate priors (Beta–Binomial, Gamma–Poisson) keep the posterior in the same family — closed-form updates.
  • The prior washes out as data grows.
  • You get credible intervals and honest uncertainty, not just an estimate.
The single hook: when data is scarce or uncertainty matters (bandits, A/B, forecasting), reasoning over the whole posterior beats a single number.

Partial derivatives & the Jacobian

A loss has many inputs. Vary one and freeze the rest — that's a partial. Stack them into the gradient, and into the Jacobian for vector outputs.

~20 min readNumPy paired notebookPrereq: Derivatives, Dot product

A real loss doesn't depend on one weight — it depends on millions, all at once. Session 8's single-variable slope has to grow up. The partial derivative is how: to find the slope in the "$w_5$ direction," you nudge $w_5$ and hold every other weight frozen. Do that for each input and stack the answers into a vector, and you've built the gradient — the exact object backpropagation computes and gradient descent follows.

This session turns "the slope" into "the slope in every direction," and introduces the Jacobian — the matrix that appears the moment your function outputs a vector, which is what makes the chain rule (Session 10) tick.

By the end you will be able to
  • Compute a partial derivative — vary one input, hold the rest constant.
  • Assemble partials into the gradient $\nabla f$ and know it's perpendicular to the level curves.
  • Use the directional derivative $\nabla f\cdot\mathbf{u}$ and see why the gradient is the steepest direction.
  • Read the Jacobian as the matrix of all partials of a vector-valued function.
  • Connect all of it to loss gradients and backprop.

Section 1Partial derivatives — one knob at a time 📖 MML §5.2

For a function of several inputs like $f(x,y)$, the partial derivative $\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}$ is just the ordinary derivative in the $x$ direction, treating $y$ as a constant:

$$ f(x,y)=x^2+y^2 \;\Rightarrow\; \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}=2x, \quad \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}=2y $$

Freeze $y$, differentiate in $x$ as usual (so $y^2$ is a constant and vanishes); then freeze $x$ and do $y$. Each partial answers "if I move along this axis only, how fast does $f$ change?" In the heat-map below, drag the point and read both partials plus the gradient arrow.

Your Power BI / SQL anchor It's a "hold-all-else-equal" sensitivity: how much does revenue change if I move price alone, keeping units and cost fixed? A partial derivative is that ceteris-paribus slope, one variable at a time. ML asks it for every parameter simultaneously.

Section 2The gradient — assembled from partials

Collect the partials into a vector and you have the gradient:

$$ \nabla f = \left[\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1},\ \dots,\ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n}\right] $$

Three facts to internalise (all visible in the demo):

  • It points in the direction of steepest ascent — the fastest way to increase $f$.
  • Its magnitude $\lVert\nabla f\rVert$ is the steepness of that climb (0 at a flat critical point).
  • It is perpendicular to the level curves (contours of constant $f$) — moving along a contour doesn't change $f$, so the steepest change is across them.
Why it matters Gradient descent updates every parameter at once: $\mathbf{w}\leftarrow\mathbf{w}-\eta\,\nabla L(\mathbf{w})$. The gradient is the object being computed billions of times in training. Everything about optimization is "find $\nabla L$, step against it."

Section 3The directional derivative

What if you want the slope in some arbitrary direction $\mathbf{u}$ (a unit vector), not just along an axis? It's the dot product of the gradient with that direction — Session 2 returning:

$$ D_\mathbf{u}f = \nabla f \cdot \mathbf{u} = \lVert\nabla f\rVert\cos\theta $$
Going deeper — why the gradient is the steepest direction

The directional derivative $\nabla f\cdot\mathbf{u} = \lVert\nabla f\rVert\lVert\mathbf{u}\rVert\cos\theta = \lVert\nabla f\rVert\cos\theta$ (since $\mathbf{u}$ is a unit vector). This is largest when $\cos\theta=1$, i.e. when $\mathbf{u}$ points the same way as $\nabla f$. So among all directions, the gradient gives the maximum rate of increase — that's the precise meaning of "steepest ascent." When $\mathbf{u}\perp\nabla f$ ($\cos\theta=0$) the slope is zero — that's why you move along a contour without changing $f$. This is the Cauchy–Schwarz bound from Session 2 doing real work. See MML §5.2.

Section 4The Jacobian — when the output is a vector 📖 MML §5.3

So far $f$ output a single number. But a neural-network layer maps a vector to a vector, $\mathbf{f}:\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}^m$. Its derivative is a whole matrix of partials — the Jacobian $J$, with one row per output and one column per input:

$$ J = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{\partial f_1}{\partial x_1} & \cdots & \frac{\partial f_1}{\partial x_n} \\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ \frac{\partial f_m}{\partial x_1} & \cdots & \frac{\partial f_m}{\partial x_n} \end{bmatrix} \quad (m\times n) $$

It's the natural generalization: a scalar output ($m=1$) makes the Jacobian a single row — exactly the gradient (transposed). The Jacobian is the local linear approximation of $\mathbf{f}$: near a point, $\mathbf{f}$ behaves like the matrix $J$ (Session 3's transformations, now as the best linear fit to a curved map).

Going deeper — Jacobians and the chain rule (backprop preview)

When you compose functions — which is exactly what a deep network does — their derivatives compose by multiplying Jacobians: $\;\frac{\partial (\mathbf{f}\circ\mathbf{g})}{\partial \mathbf{x}} = J_\mathbf{f}\,J_\mathbf{g}$. A network is a long chain of layers, so its overall Jacobian is a product of per-layer Jacobians. Backpropagation is nothing more than evaluating that product efficiently, right-to-left, so a scalar loss's gradient flows back through every layer. The second-derivative analogue, the Hessian $H$ (matrix of $\partial^2 f/\partial x_i\partial x_j$), captures curvature and underlies Newton's method. See MML §5.3; the mechanics are Session 10.

Section 5In NumPy ✓ outputs verified

import numpy as np

def f(p):                      # f(x,y) = x^2 + y^2
    x, y = p
    return x**2 + y**2

def grad(f, p, h=1e-5):        # numerical gradient: one partial per input
    p = np.asarray(p, float); g = np.zeros_like(p)
    for i in range(len(p)):
        step = np.zeros_like(p); step[i] = h
        g[i] = (f(p+step) - f(p-step)) / (2*h)
    return g

print(grad(f, [3.0, 4.0]))    # [6. 8.]  == [2x, 2y]
print(np.linalg.norm(grad(f, [3., 4.])))  # 10.0  steepness
[6. 8.] 10.0
In real code, autodiff builds the Jacobian for you The loop above is a fine gradient check, but frameworks compute exact gradients and Jacobians automatically: PyTorch's torch.autograd.functional.jacobian, or just loss.backward() to get ∂loss/∂params. Understanding the Jacobian tells you what those calls return and why shapes line up.

Section 6Where it lives in real ML

  • The loss gradient. $\nabla_\mathbf{w}L$ has one partial per parameter — the update direction for every weight, computed each step.
  • Backprop = chained Jacobians. Each layer contributes a Jacobian; the gradient is their product evaluated backward (Session 10).
  • Contours & optimization paths. Because the gradient is ⟂ to contours, descent zig-zags across them — and elongated contours (ill-conditioning) cause slow zig-zagging, motivating momentum/Adam.
  • Sensitivity analysis. Partials tell you which inputs a model is most sensitive to (feature attribution, saliency maps).
The one-line takeaway A partial is a slope in one direction; the gradient stacks them and points steepest uphill (⟂ to contours); the Jacobian stacks gradients for vector outputs. Backprop multiplies Jacobians — which is the next session.
Computational & memory cost A gradient has one entry per parameter — $O(\#\text{params})$ to store, and (via reverse-mode autodiff) obtainable in a single backward pass. A full Jacobian of an $m$-output, $n$-input map is $m\times n$: $O(mn)$ to store, which is why we rarely form it explicitly and use Jacobian–vector products instead.

ReferenceEdge cases, rules & traps

Edge cases & gotchas

  • Gradient is a vector, not a number — one component per input. Its direction and magnitude both carry meaning.
  • $\nabla f=\mathbf{0}$ marks a critical point (min, max, or saddle) — in high dimensions, saddles vastly outnumber minima.
  • Jacobian shape is $m\times n$ (outputs × inputs). Getting rows/columns backwards is the classic backprop shape bug.
  • Scalar output ⇒ Jacobian is $1\times n$ = $\nabla f^\top$; people use "gradient" and "Jacobian" loosely — check the shape.

Golden rules

  1. A partial $\partial f/\partial x_i$ = slope in one input, others held constant.
  2. The gradient stacks the partials; it points steepest ascent and is ⟂ to level curves.
  3. Directional derivative $=\nabla f\cdot\mathbf{u}$; maximal along the gradient (Cauchy–Schwarz).
  4. The Jacobian ($m\times n$) is the matrix of partials of a vector-valued function; scalar output ⇒ it's the gradient row.
  5. Backprop multiplies Jacobians down the network (Session 10).
Trap 1 · Treating the gradient as a scalar $\nabla f$ is a vector living in input space. Its length is the steepness, its direction is which way is up — you lose half the information if you only look at magnitude.
Trap 2 · Jacobian row/column confusion Rows = outputs, columns = inputs → shape $m\times n$. Transposing it by accident is the #1 cause of shape errors in hand-written backprop.
Trap 3 · Assuming ∇f = 0 means a minimum In high dimensions it's usually a saddle. That's why plain gradient descent stalls and momentum/adaptive methods help (Session 11).

E4medium

At $(1,1)$ on $f=x^2+y^2$, compute the directional derivative along $\mathbf{u}=[1,0]$ and along the gradient direction. Which is larger, and why?

Show solution

$\nabla f=[2,2]$. Along $[1,0]$: $\nabla f\cdot[1,0]=2$. Along $\hat{\nabla f}=[1,1]/\sqrt2$: $\nabla f\cdot\hat{\nabla f}=\lVert\nabla f\rVert=\sqrt8\approx2.83$ — larger, because the gradient direction is steepest.

E5hard

The map $\mathbf{f}(x,y)=[x^2y,\ x+\sin y]$ goes ℝ²→ℝ². Write its 2×2 Jacobian by hand and evaluate at $(1,0)$.

Show solution

$J=\begin{bmatrix}2xy & x^2\\ 1 & \cos y\end{bmatrix}$. At $(1,0)$: $\begin{bmatrix}0 & 1\\ 1 & 1\end{bmatrix}$.

h=1e-5; F=lambda x,y:np.array([x*x*y, x+np.sin(y)])

InterviewQuestions you should be able to answer

Q1 · What's a partial derivative?
The derivative with respect to one variable, holding the others constant — the slope of the function along that single axis.
Q2 · What does the gradient represent, and how does it relate to contours?
The vector of all partials; it points in the direction of steepest ascent, its length is the steepness, and it's perpendicular to the level curves of the function.
Q3 · Why is the gradient the direction of steepest ascent?
The directional derivative $\nabla f\cdot\mathbf{u}=\lVert\nabla f\rVert\cos\theta$ is maximized when $\mathbf{u}$ aligns with $\nabla f$ (Cauchy–Schwarz), so no direction increases $f$ faster.
Q4 · What is the Jacobian and its shape?
The matrix of all partials of a vector-valued function $\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}^m$; shape $m\times n$ (outputs × inputs). For scalar output it's the gradient (as a row).
Q5 · How does the Jacobian connect to backprop?
Composed functions' derivatives multiply their Jacobians; a network is a composition, so backprop evaluates the product of per-layer Jacobians backward to get the loss gradient.

◆ Concept Card
The gradient points uphill in every dimension at once.

Reusable idea: Partial derivatives measure slope in each axis; stacked, they form the gradient vector.

  • ∇f points in the direction of steepest ascent; $-\nabla f$ is steepest descent.
  • The Jacobian stacks gradients of a vector output.
  • Gradient magnitude = how steep; direction = where.
The single hook: the gradient is the compass of optimization — every parameter update reads it to decide where to step.

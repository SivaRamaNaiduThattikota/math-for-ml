Matrices as transformations

A matrix isn't a spreadsheet — it's a machine that moves space. Rotations, stretches, and projections are all one grid of numbers doing its job.

~20 min readNumPy paired notebookPrereq: Vectors, Dot product

You've been treating a matrix as a spreadsheet — a grid of numbers. That view is correct but useless for intuition. The view that unlocks machine learning is this: a matrix is a function that moves space. Feed it a vector, it hands back a transformed vector — rotated, stretched, sheared, or flattened. A neural-network layer, a rotation in PCA, an image warp, a change of coordinates: all of them are a matrix doing the same one thing — transforming every point at once, linearly.

Once you see matrices as transformations rather than tables, matrix multiplication (next session) stops being an arbitrary rule and becomes obvious: it's just doing one transformation after another.

By the end you will be able to
  • Read a matrix as a linear transformation — a machine that maps every vector to a new one.
  • Use the one mental model that explains everything: the columns are where the basis vectors land.
  • Compute a matrix–vector product two ways — as a linear combination of columns, and as a stack of dot products.
  • Recognize the transformation zoo: scaling, rotation, shear, reflection, projection.
  • Read the determinant as the factor by which area (or volume) is scaled — and what a negative or zero determinant means.

Section 1A matrix is a machine that moves space 📖 MML §2.7

A matrix $A$ takes a vector in and gives a vector out: $\mathbf{x} \mapsto A\mathbf{x}$. Apply it to every point in the plane and the whole space transforms — but in a very disciplined way. Watch what a matrix does to the humble vector $[1,1]$:

$$ A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 3 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad A\begin{bmatrix}1\\1\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2\cdot1 + 1\cdot1 \\ 0\cdot1 + 3\cdot1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}3\\3\end{bmatrix} $$
Your Power BI / SQL anchor In Power BI you built a matrix as a table — rows and columns of numbers. That's a valid storage view. But the operation that makes matrices powerful isn't "look up a cell" — it's "apply this whole grid to a vector and get a transformed vector." Same object, a completely different verb: from storing numbers to transforming them.

Section 2The columns tell you everything

Here is the single idea that makes linear algebra click. The columns of a matrix are where the basis vectors land. Take the standard basis $\hat{\imath}=[1,0]$ and $\hat{\jmath}=[0,1]$. After applying $A$:

$$ A = \begin{bmatrix} \color{#5A4FCF}2 & \color{#0E8C80}1 \\ \color{#5A4FCF}0 & \color{#0E8C80}3 \end{bmatrix} \;\Rightarrow\; \hat{\imath}\mapsto\begin{bmatrix}\color{#5A4FCF}2\\\color{#5A4FCF}0\end{bmatrix},\quad \hat{\jmath}\mapsto\begin{bmatrix}\color{#0E8C80}1\\\color{#0E8C80}3\end{bmatrix} $$

The first column is the new home of $\hat{\imath}$; the second column is the new home of $\hat{\jmath}$. That's all a matrix stores: the destinations of the basis vectors. Everything else follows, because every vector is just a combination of $\hat{\imath}$ and $\hat{\jmath}$ — and a linear transformation keeps that combination intact.

Section 3Matrix × vector, two ways 📖 MML §2.2

Because a vector $[x,y]$ means "$x$ steps along $\hat{\imath}$, $y$ steps along $\hat{\jmath}$," and the matrix tells us where those land, the transformed vector is the same combination of the columns. This is the column view — the one worth burning into memory:

$$ A\mathbf{x} = x\,(\text{column 1}) + y\,(\text{column 2}) = x\begin{bmatrix}2\\0\end{bmatrix} + y\begin{bmatrix}1\\3\end{bmatrix} $$

There's a second, equally valid row view: each entry of the output is a dot product of a row of $A$ with $\mathbf{x}$ — which ties this session straight back to Session 2:

$$ A\mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} \text{row}_1 \cdot \mathbf{x} \\ \text{row}_2 \cdot \mathbf{x} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} [2,1]\cdot[x,y] \\ [0,3]\cdot[x,y] \end{bmatrix} $$
Two lenses, one operationThe column view ("combination of column vectors") builds geometric intuition; the row view ("stack of dot products") is how you compute by hand and how code loops. Fluent people switch between them mid-sentence.
Why it matters The row view is why Session 2 mattered: a matrix–vector product is a batch of dot products. A neural-network layer computing $W\mathbf{x}$ is running one dot product per output neuron. Hold that thought — Session 4 stacks it into full matrix multiplication.

Section 4The transformation zoo

A handful of matrices show up everywhere. Each is just a choice of "where do the basis vectors go?"

Section 5What makes a transformation "linear"?

Not every way of moving space is a matrix. A transformation is linear — and therefore representable as a matrix — exactly when it keeps grid lines straight, parallel, and evenly spaced, and keeps the origin fixed. Curving, bending, or shifting the origin is not linear (that last one is why bias terms $+\mathbf{b}$ are handled separately in a neural net — they're the non-linear-in-this-sense part).

Going deeper — the formal definition of linearity

A map $T:\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}^m$ is linear if it respects addition and scalar multiplication — for all vectors $\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}$ and scalars $\lambda$:

$$ T(\mathbf{x}+\mathbf{y}) = T(\mathbf{x}) + T(\mathbf{y}) \qquad\text{and}\qquad T(\lambda\mathbf{x}) = \lambda\,T(\mathbf{x}) $$

These two conditions are exactly why "the columns are where the basis vectors land" works: any $\mathbf{x}=x\hat{\imath}+y\hat{\jmath}$ maps to $T(\mathbf{x}) = xT(\hat{\imath}) + yT(\hat{\jmath})$ — knowing where the basis goes determines the whole map. Every linear map between finite-dimensional spaces is a matrix once you fix a basis. Formal treatment: MML §2.7.

Section 6The determinant — how much area changes 📖 MML §4.1

Apply a matrix and the unit square becomes a parallelogram (Figure 1). The determinant is the factor by which area is scaled:

$$ \det\!\begin{bmatrix}a&b\\c&d\end{bmatrix} = ad - bc $$
  • $\det = 2$ → areas double. $\det = 1$ → area preserved (e.g. any rotation).
  • $\det < 0$ → space was flipped (mirrored); the magnitude is still the area factor.
  • $\det = 0$ → space was collapsed onto a line or point — a dimension was lost. The matrix is singular (non-invertible): you can't undo a transformation that threw information away.
Going deeper — why det = 0 means "not invertible"

If a transformation squashes 2-D space onto a line, many different input points get mapped to the same output point — so there's no way to reverse it uniquely. Algebraically, that's when the columns are linearly dependent (one is a multiple of the other), the matrix has rank less than full, and $\det = 0$. Rank, independence, and invertibility are the whole subject of Session 5 — the determinant is your first detector for them. See MML §4.1.

Section 7In NumPy — and where it lives in ML

import numpy as np

A = np.array([[2, 1], [0, 3]], dtype=float)
x = np.array([1, 1], dtype=float)

print(A @ x)                # [3. 3.]  apply the transformation
print(np.linalg.det(A))     # 6.0     area scales x6
print(np.eye(2) @ x)        # [1. 1.]  identity = do nothing

# the columns are the images of the basis vectors
print(A @ np.array([1,0]))   # [2. 0.]  -> column 1
print(A @ np.array([0,1]))   # [1. 3.]  -> column 2
[3. 3.] 6.0 [1. 1.] [2. 0.] [1. 3.]
  • Every neural-network layer is a linear transformation $W\mathbf{x}$ followed by a bias and a nonlinearity. The weight matrix is a learned transformation of the feature space.
  • PCA (Session 7) applies a rotation matrix that aligns the axes with the data's directions of greatest variance.
  • Embeddings & projections — mapping a 768-D vector down to 2-D for a plot is a (linear) projection matrix.
  • Image processing & graphics — rotations, scaling, and shears of images are literally these matrices.

ReferenceEdge cases, rules & traps

Edge cases & gotchas

  • Shape rule: for $A\mathbf{x}$, the number of columns of $A$ must equal the length of $\mathbf{x}$. An $m\times n$ matrix maps $\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}^m$.
  • Order matters (next session): transformations don't generally commute — rotating then shearing ≠ shearing then rotating.
  • The origin is fixed: a pure matrix always sends $\mathbf{0}\to\mathbf{0}$. Moving the origin needs a separate translation (a bias vector) — that's an affine, not linear, map.
  • Non-square matrices change the dimension (e.g. $2\times3$ maps 3-D → 2-D) — they can't have a determinant.

Golden rules

  1. A matrix is a linear transformation of space — read it as a verb, not a table.
  2. The columns are where the basis vectors land. That's the whole matrix.
  3. $A\mathbf{x}$ = a linear combination of the columns (also = a stack of row·x dot products).
  4. $\det$ = area/volume scaling; sign = orientation; zero = collapsed & non-invertible.
  5. An $m\times n$ matrix maps $\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}^m$ — match columns to input length.
Trap 1 · Reading a matrix by rows for intuition For computing $A\mathbf{x}$ by hand, rows-as-dot-products is fine. For understanding what a matrix does, think columns — where the basis lands. Mixing them up is the #1 source of "I can compute it but I don't get it."
Trap 2 · Forgetting the origin is pinned A linear map can't translate. If your transformation needs to move the origin (shift data), that's a bias/affine step, handled separately — a classic bug when hand-rolling transforms.
Trap 3 · Assuming order doesn't matter Matrix (transformation) composition is not commutative. "Rotate then scale" and "scale then rotate" give different results — Session 4 makes this precise.

PracticeExercises

Hints only — solve in the paired practice.ipynb, predicting each result geometrically first.

E1easy

Apply $A=\begin{bmatrix}3&0\\0&1\end{bmatrix}$ to $[2,5]$ by hand. Describe what the matrix does in words.

Show solution

$A[2,5] = [6,5]$. It stretches the x-axis by 3 and leaves y unchanged (an axis-aligned scaling).

np.array([[3,0],[0,1]]) @ np.array([2,5])  # [6 5]
E2easy

What are the images of $\hat{\imath}$ and $\hat{\jmath}$ under $\begin{bmatrix}1&1\\0&1\end{bmatrix}$? What transformation is this?

Show solution

$\hat{\imath}\mapsto[1,0]$ (unchanged), $\hat{\jmath}\mapsto[1,1]$ (slanted right). It's a horizontal shear.

E3medium

Build the 2×2 matrix that rotates 90° counter-clockwise, then verify it sends $[1,0]\to[0,1]$.

Show solution

Rotation by 90°: $\hat{\imath}\to[0,1]$, $\hat{\jmath}\to[-1,0]$, so $R=\begin{bmatrix}0&-1\\1&0\end{bmatrix}$.

R = np.array([[0,-1],[1,0]])
R @ np.array([1,0])   # [0 1]  ✓
E4medium

Compute $\det$ of $\begin{bmatrix}2&1\\4&2\end{bmatrix}$. What does the result tell you geometrically?

Show solution

$\det = (2)(2)-(1)(4) = 0$. The columns $[2,4]$ and $[1,2]$ are parallel (one is 2× the other), so the matrix collapses the plane onto a line — singular, non-invertible.

E5hard

A projection onto the x-axis is $P=\begin{bmatrix}1&0\\0&0\end{bmatrix}$. Show that applying it twice equals applying it once ($P^2 = P$), and explain why that makes sense.

Show solution
P = np.array([[1,0],[0,0]])
P @ P            # [[1 0],[0 0]] == P

Once you've flattened a point onto the x-axis, flattening again does nothing — it's already there. Transformations with $P^2=P$ are called idempotent (all projections are). $\det P = 0$, confirming it loses a dimension.

InterviewQuestions you should be able to answer

Q1 · What does a matrix represent, geometrically?
A linear transformation of space — it maps every vector to a new vector, keeping grid lines straight/parallel/evenly spaced and the origin fixed. Its columns are the images of the basis vectors.
Q2 · Give the two ways to read a matrix–vector product.
Column view: a linear combination of the matrix's columns weighted by the vector's entries. Row view: a stack of dot products of each row with the vector. Same result; one for intuition, one for computation.
Q3 · What does the determinant tell you?
The factor by which area/volume is scaled. Negative = orientation flipped; zero = space collapsed to a lower dimension, so the matrix is singular (non-invertible).
Q4 · Why is det = 0 the same as "not invertible"?
A zero determinant means the columns are linearly dependent and a dimension was lost; multiple inputs map to the same output, so the map can't be uniquely reversed.
Q5 · How does this connect to neural networks?
A layer computes $W\mathbf{x}+\mathbf{b}$: the weight matrix is a learned linear transformation of the input space, the bias is a translation, and a nonlinearity follows. Training learns the transformation.

A full distribution (Session 13) is rich but unwieldy. Most of the time you want a few numbers that summarize it: where is it centered, how spread out is it, and — for several variables — how do they move together? Those are expectation, variance, and covariance. They're the quantities behind feature standardization, the covariance matrix that drives PCA, correlation-based feature selection, and the bias–variance trade-off that governs over- and under-fitting.

Three summary numbers, endlessly reused. This session makes each precise and connects the covariance matrix straight back to the PCA/SVD ellipse.

By the end you will be able to
  • Compute an expectation (mean) as a weighted average, and use its linearity.
  • Compute variance and standard deviation, and use $\operatorname{Var}(X)=E[X^2]-E[X]^2$.
  • Interpret covariance and scale-free correlation $\rho\in[-1,1]$.
  • Read a covariance matrix and connect it to PCA.
  • Tie these to standardization, whitening, feature selection, and bias–variance.

Section 1Expectation — the mean as a weighted average 📖 MML §6.4

The expectation $E[X]$ is the long-run average value of $X$, each value weighted by its probability:

$$ E[X]=\sum_x x\,P(x)\quad(\text{discrete}), \qquad E[X]=\int x\,p(x)\,dx\quad(\text{continuous}) $$

A fair die: $E[X]=\tfrac16(1+2+\dots+6)=3.5$. The single most useful property is linearity — it holds always, even for dependent variables:

$$ E[aX+b]=aE[X]+b, \qquad E[X+Y]=E[X]+E[Y] $$
Your Power BI / SQL anchor $E[X]$ is AVG(x) — but the weighted version, SUM(value * probability). Every "expected revenue = Σ outcome × probability" calculation you've built is an expectation. Variance below is just VAR()/STDEV(), and covariance is the two-column generalization.

Section 2Variance & standard deviation

Variance measures spread — the average squared distance from the mean:

$$ \operatorname{Var}(X)=E\big[(X-\mu)^2\big]=E[X^2]-E[X]^2 $$

The second form ("mean of the square minus square of the mean") is the handy computational one. The standard deviation $\sigma=\sqrt{\operatorname{Var}(X)}$ puts spread back in the original units. Two scaling facts you'll reuse constantly:

$$ \operatorname{Var}(aX)=a^2\operatorname{Var}(X), \qquad \operatorname{Var}(X+Y)=\operatorname{Var}(X)+\operatorname{Var}(Y)+2\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y) $$

That $a^2$ is why standardizing divides by $\sigma$ (not $\sigma^2$), and the second identity is the seed of the Central Limit Theorem (Session 15).

Section 3Covariance & correlation 📖 MML §6.4

Covariance asks whether two variables rise and fall together:

$$ \operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)=E\big[(X-\mu_X)(Y-\mu_Y)\big] $$
  • Positive → they tend to move the same way; negative → opposite; ≈ 0 → no linear relationship.

Covariance's size depends on units, which makes it hard to interpret. Correlation fixes that by normalizing to $[-1,1]$:

$$ \rho = \frac{\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)}{\sigma_X\,\sigma_Y}\ \in[-1,1] $$

$\rho=\pm1$ means a perfect line; $\rho=0$ means uncorrelated. Drag the correlation below and watch the cloud tilt — and its covariance ellipse rotate with it.

Section 4The covariance matrix 📖 MML §6.5

For a random vector $\mathbf{x}=[X_1,\dots,X_n]$, all the pairwise covariances collect into the covariance matrix $\Sigma$, where $\Sigma_{ij}=\operatorname{Cov}(X_i,X_j)$:

$$ \Sigma = \begin{bmatrix} \operatorname{Var}(X_1) & \operatorname{Cov}(X_1,X_2) & \cdots \\ \operatorname{Cov}(X_2,X_1) & \operatorname{Var}(X_2) & \cdots \\ \vdots & & \ddots \end{bmatrix} $$

The diagonal holds the variances, the off-diagonal the covariances, and it's always symmetric and positive-semidefinite. That last fact is the whole reason PCA works.

Going deeper — variance of a sum, and why independence lets variances add

Expanding the square gives $\operatorname{Var}(X+Y)=\operatorname{Var}(X)+\operatorname{Var}(Y)+2\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)$. If $X$ and $Y$ are independent (or just uncorrelated), $\operatorname{Cov}=0$, so variances simply add:

$$ \operatorname{Var}\!\Big(\sum_i X_i\Big)=\sum_i \operatorname{Var}(X_i) \quad(\text{independent}) $$

This is why averaging $n$ independent samples cuts variance by $n$ (the mean's variance is $\sigma^2/n$) — the mathematical engine of the Central Limit Theorem (Session 15) and of variance reduction by ensembling/bagging in ML. And since $\Sigma$ is symmetric PSD, its eigenvectors (Session 6) are orthogonal and its eigenvalues non-negative — those eigenvectors are exactly the principal components, eigenvalues the variances along them (Session 7). See MML §6.4–6.5.

Section 5In NumPy ✓ outputs verified

import numpy as np

x = np.array([2, 4, 4, 4, 5, 5, 7, 9.])
print(x.mean(), x.var(), x.std())      # mean 5.0, var 4.0, std 2.0

# E[X^2] - E[X]^2 == Var(X)
print((x**2).mean() - x.mean()**2)     # 4.0

# covariance matrix & correlation of two features
a = np.random.normal(size=1000)
b = 0.7*a + 0.3*np.random.normal(size=1000)
print(np.cov(a, b).round(2))            # [[~1, ~0.7],[~0.7, ~0.6]]
print(np.corrcoef(a, b)[0,1].round(2))  # ~0.9  (scale-free)
5.0 4.0 2.0 4.0 [[1.02 0.71] [0.71 0.58]] 0.92
n vs n−1, and the axis NumPy's var/std default to dividing by $n$ (population); use ddof=1 for the sample estimate ($n-1$, unbiased) when estimating from data. And np.cov expects variables as rows by default — transpose a samples×features matrix (or pass rowvar=False).

Section 6Where it lives in real ML

  • Standardization — $z=(x-\mu)/\sigma$ uses exactly the mean and std; it's Session 1's feature scaling, now named.
  • The covariance matrix drives PCA (eigenvectors = components), whitening (decorrelate + unit-variance), and the Mahalanobis distance for anomaly detection.
  • Correlation flags redundant features (multicollinearity, Session 5) and guides feature selection — but remember correlation ≠ causation.
  • Bias–variance trade-off — a model's error decomposes into bias² + variance; ensembling/bagging reduces the variance term by averaging (the "variances add / average" fact above).
The one-line takeaway Expectation is the center, variance the spread, covariance/correlation how variables move together. Stack the covariances into $\Sigma$ — symmetric PSD — and its eigenvectors are the PCA axes. These three numbers underlie standardization, PCA, feature selection, and bias–variance.

ReferenceEdge cases, rules & traps

Edge cases & gotchas

  • Uncorrelated ≠ independent: $\rho=0$ only rules out a linear relationship; a U-shaped dependence has zero correlation.
  • Correlation ≠ causation: a strong $\rho$ can come from a confounder, not a causal link.
  • Population vs sample: divide by $n$ for the population variance, $n-1$ (ddof=1) for an unbiased sample estimate.
  • Outliers wreck variance/covariance — they're squared, so a few extremes dominate. Consider robust stats when needed.

Golden rules

  1. Expectation = probability-weighted average; linearity $E[X+Y]=E[X]+E[Y]$ always holds.
  2. Variance $=E[X^2]-E[X]^2$; std $=\sqrt{\text{Var}}$; $\operatorname{Var}(aX)=a^2\operatorname{Var}(X)$.
  3. Covariance = how two move together; correlation $\rho=\operatorname{Cov}/(\sigma_X\sigma_Y)\in[-1,1]$.
  4. The covariance matrix $\Sigma$ is symmetric PSD; diagonal = variances; eigenvectors = PCA directions.
  5. Independent ⇒ $\operatorname{Cov}=0$ ⇒ variances add (basis of the CLT).
Trap 1 · Correlation ≠ causation, and ρ=0 ≠ independent Zero correlation only means no linear link; nonlinear dependence can still be strong. And a high correlation never proves one variable causes the other.
Trap 2 · ddof and rowvar in np.cov np.cov uses $n-1$ by default and treats rows as variables. For a samples×features array, pass rowvar=False or you'll compute the covariance of the wrong axis.
Trap 3 · Outliers dominate squared stats Because deviations are squared, a handful of outliers can blow up variance and covariance. Inspect/clip outliers or use robust alternatives before trusting them.

Make $Y=2X+\text{noise}$; compute $\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)$ and $\rho$. Then scale $X$ by 1000 — which changes, covariance or correlation?

Correlation is scale-free (unchanged); covariance scales with the units (changes ×1000). That's why we usually report $\rho$.

E4medium

Show that averaging $n$ independent samples reduces variance: compare the variance of single draws to the variance of means of 25 draws.

Show solution
singles=np.random.normal(size=100000)
means=np.random.normal(size=(100000,25)).mean(1)
singles.var(), means.var()   # ~1.0 vs ~0.04  (= 1/25)

Variance of the mean is $\sigma^2/n=1/25\approx0.04$ — the CLT preview.

E5hard

Build a 2-D correlated cloud, compute its covariance matrix, and confirm the top eigenvector points along the cloud's main axis.

Show solution
a=np.random.normal(size=2000); b=0.9*a+0.4*np.random.normal(size=2000)
S=np.cov(np.vstack([a,b]))
w,V=np.linalg.eigh(S)
V[:,-1]   # top eigenvector ~ along [1, ~0.9] (the cloud's tilt)

The eigenvector for the largest eigenvalue aligns with the cloud's greatest-spread direction — PCA's first component.

InterviewQuestions you should be able to answer

Q1 · Define expectation and variance.
Expectation is the probability-weighted average $E[X]=\sum x P(x)$. Variance is the average squared deviation $E[(X-\mu)^2]=E[X^2]-E[X]^2$; std dev is its square root.
Q2 · Covariance vs correlation?
Covariance measures joint variation but depends on units; correlation normalizes it by the two std devs into $[-1,1]$, making it scale-free and comparable.
Q3 · Does zero correlation imply independence?
No — it only rules out a linear relationship. Independent ⇒ uncorrelated, but uncorrelated variables can be nonlinearly dependent.
Q4 · What's special about the covariance matrix?
It's symmetric and positive-semidefinite, so it has real non-negative eigenvalues and orthogonal eigenvectors — which are the PCA directions (variance = eigenvalue along each).
Q5 · Why does averaging reduce variance?
For independent samples, variances add, so the mean of $n$ has variance $\sigma^2/n$. This underlies the CLT and variance reduction via bagging/ensembling.

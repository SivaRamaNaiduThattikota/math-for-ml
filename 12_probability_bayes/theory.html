Probability & Bayes

Reasoning under uncertainty. The sum and product rules, and Bayes' theorem — posterior ∝ likelihood × prior — the engine of updating beliefs.

~20 min readNumPy paired notebookPrereq: none (probability start)

Linear algebra moved data; calculus optimized it. But models are never certain — they predict probabilities, learn from noisy data, and must reason about what they haven't seen. Probability is the language for that uncertainty, and Bayes' theorem is its engine: a precise rule for updating what you believe when new evidence arrives. Spam filters, medical-risk models, Naive Bayes, and the whole Bayesian view of machine learning are this one formula, applied.

This session opens the probability pillar with the handful of rules everything rests on — and the famous, deeply counter-intuitive lesson of the base rate.

By the end you will be able to
  • Use the basic rules of probability — sample space, events, and the axioms.
  • Work with joint, marginal, and conditional probability via the sum and product rules.
  • Test for independence and understand conditional independence (Naive Bayes).
  • Apply Bayes' theorem: posterior ∝ likelihood × prior.
  • Explain the base-rate effect — why a 99%-accurate test can still be usually wrong.

Notation reference
$P(A)$Probability of event $A$ — a number in $[0,1]$. $P(A,B)$Joint probability — $A$ and $B$ both happen. $P(A\mid B)$Conditional — probability of $A$ given $B$ is known. $\perp\!\!\!\perp$Independent: $P(A,B)=P(A)P(B)$. $P(H\mid E)$Posterior — belief in hypothesis $H$ after evidence $E$.

Section 1Probability basics 📖 MML §6.1

Start with a sample space $\Omega$ — all possible outcomes (for a die, $\{1,2,3,4,5,6\}$). An event is a subset of outcomes ("even" $=\{2,4,6\}$). A probability $P$ assigns each event a number obeying three axioms:

  • $0 \le P(A) \le 1$ — probabilities live between impossible and certain.
  • $P(\Omega) = 1$ — something must happen.
  • For mutually exclusive events, $P(A\text{ or }B)=P(A)+P(B)$ — disjoint chances add.

Everything else — conditional probability, Bayes, entire probabilistic models — is built from these three lines.

Your Power BI / SQL anchor Probability is just normalized counting. $P(A)$ = (rows where $A$) ÷ (total rows) — a COUNT(*) FILTER (WHERE A) / COUNT(*). A conditional $P(A\mid B)$ restricts the denominator to rows where $B$ holds. You've been computing probabilities every time you wrote a rate or a percentage.

Section 2Joint, marginal & conditional 📖 MML §6.3

With two variables you get three related quantities, tied together by two rules:

  • Joint $P(A,B)$ — both happen.
  • Marginal $P(A)$ — one alone, obtained by the sum rule (marginalizing out $B$): $\;P(A)=\sum_b P(A,b)$.
  • Conditional $P(A\mid B)$ — $A$ given $B$, defined by the product rule:
$$ P(A\mid B) = \frac{P(A,B)}{P(B)} \qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad P(A,B) = P(A\mid B)\,P(B) $$

Conditioning "zooms in" on the world where $B$ is true and renormalizes. The sum rule collapses a dimension; the product rule factors a joint into a chain of conditionals — together they're the entire algebra of probability.

Section 3Independence 📖 MML §6.4

Two events are independent when knowing one tells you nothing about the other:

$$ P(A,B)=P(A)\,P(B) \qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad P(A\mid B)=P(A) $$

Coin flips are independent; a card's suit and rank are independent; today's and tomorrow's weather are not. A subtler, ML-critical idea is conditional independence: $A$ and $B$ may be dependent overall but independent once you know $C$. The Naive Bayes classifier is built on assuming features are conditionally independent given the class — "naive," often false, yet remarkably effective (spam filters).

Section 4Bayes' theorem 📖 MML §6.3

Because $P(A,B)$ can be factored two ways — $P(A\mid B)P(B)=P(B\mid A)P(A)$ — you can flip a conditional around. That's Bayes' theorem, the rule for updating a belief with evidence:

$$ \underbrace{P(H\mid E)}_{\text{posterior}} = \frac{\overbrace{P(E\mid H)}^{\text{likelihood}}\ \overbrace{P(H)}^{\text{prior}}}{\underbrace{P(E)}_{\text{evidence}}} \qquad P(E)=P(E\mid H)P(H)+P(E\mid \neg H)P(\neg H) $$

In words: posterior ∝ likelihood × prior. Start with a prior belief in a hypothesis $H$; see evidence $E$; multiply by how likely that evidence is under $H$; renormalize. The demo below is the canonical case — a medical test — where the prior (how common the disease is) dominates the answer in a way most people find shocking.

Section 5The base rate — why 99% accurate ≠ 99% sure

Take the demo's defaults: a disease affecting 1% of people, a test with 99% sensitivity and 95% specificity. You test positive. How likely are you actually sick? Intuition screams "99%." Bayes says:

$$ P(D\mid +) = \frac{0.99\times 0.01}{\,0.99\times 0.01 + 0.05\times 0.99\,} = \frac{0.0099}{0.0594} \approx 0.167 $$

Only ≈ 17%. The reason: sick people are rare (1%), so even a small 5% false-positive rate applied to the huge healthy majority produces more false alarms than there are true cases. The prior (base rate) dominates. Ignoring it — base-rate neglect — is one of the most common and costly reasoning errors, in medicine and in ML (e.g. fraud/anomaly detection on rare positives).

Going deeper — deriving Bayes, and the "posterior ∝ likelihood × prior" shortcut

The joint factors two ways: $P(H,E)=P(H\mid E)P(E)=P(E\mid H)P(H)$. Equate the right-hand sides and divide by $P(E)$ → Bayes. Because $P(E)$ doesn't depend on $H$, when you compare hypotheses you can drop it:

$$ P(H\mid E) \;\propto\; P(E\mid H)\,P(H) $$

Compute likelihood × prior for each hypothesis, then normalize so they sum to 1. This "unnormalized posterior" is how Bayesian ML works in practice, and MAP estimation (Session 17) just picks the $H$ that maximizes it. The prior acts as a regularizer — a belief that survives unless the data strongly overrides it. See MML §6.3.

Section 6In NumPy — and where it lives in ML ✓ outputs verified

import numpy as np

# Bayes for the medical test
prev, sens, spec = 0.01, 0.99, 0.95
p_pos = sens*prev + (1-spec)*(1-prev)          # P(+)  (evidence)
post  = sens*prev / p_pos                        # P(D | +)
print(round(post, 3))                          # 0.167

# posterior ∝ likelihood × prior, then normalize (two hypotheses)
prior = np.array([0.01, 0.99])                    # [D, not-D]
like  = np.array([sens, 1-spec])                   # P(+ | each)
post  = prior*like / np.sum(prior*like)
print(post.round(3))                          # [0.167 0.833]
0.167 [0.167 0.833]
  • Naive Bayes classifiers — multiply per-feature likelihoods times the class prior (conditional-independence assumption); still a strong spam/text baseline.
  • Priors as regularization — a prior over weights biases the model toward simpler solutions; MAP estimation (Session 17) is exactly "maximize likelihood × prior."
  • Calibration & decision thresholds — turning model scores into real probabilities, and choosing thresholds, is Bayes reasoning about base rates and costs.
  • Anomaly / fraud detection — rare positives mean base rates dominate; a "high-accuracy" detector can still be mostly false alarms (this session's lesson, in production).
The one-line takeaway Probability is normalized counting; the sum and product rules are its algebra; Bayes flips a conditional to update beliefs — posterior ∝ likelihood × prior. And always respect the base rate.

ReferenceEdge cases, rules & traps

Edge cases & gotchas

  • $P(A\mid B)\ne P(B\mid A)$ — the "prosecutor's fallacy." $P(+\mid \text{sick})$ is not $P(\text{sick}\mid +)$; Bayes is exactly the correction.
  • Conditioning changes the denominator — $P(A\mid B)$ divides by $P(B)$, not by everything.
  • Rare events + imperfect tests ⇒ most positives are false. Precision on rare classes is base-rate-limited.
  • Independence is an assumption — Naive Bayes assumes it even when false; usually helps, sometimes hurts.

Golden rules

  1. Probabilities are in $[0,1]$; $P(\Omega)=1$; disjoint events add.
  2. Product rule $P(A,B)=P(A\mid B)P(B)$; sum rule $P(A)=\sum_b P(A,b)$.
  3. Independent ⟺ $P(A,B)=P(A)P(B)$; watch for conditional independence (Naive Bayes).
  4. Bayes: $P(H\mid E)=\dfrac{P(E\mid H)P(H)}{P(E)}$ — posterior ∝ likelihood × prior.
  5. Always fold in the base rate; a positive test on a rare condition is often a false alarm.
Trap 1 · Confusing P(A|B) with P(B|A) "99% of sick people test positive" does not mean "99% of positives are sick." Flipping requires Bayes and the base rate — getting this wrong misreads test results and model precision.
Trap 2 · Ignoring the base rate On rare positives (disease, fraud, defects), the prior dominates the posterior. A high-accuracy classifier can still have low precision. Report precision/recall, not just accuracy (your fraud project's lesson).
Trap 3 · Assuming independence blindly Multiplying probabilities assumes independence. Correlated features break Naive Bayes' math even if predictions stay usable — know it's an approximation.

PracticeExercises

Hints only — solve in the paired practice.ipynb.

E1easy

A fair die is rolled. $P(\text{even})$? $P(\text{even}\mid \text{>3})$? Are "even" and ">3" independent?

Show solution

$P(\text{even})=3/6=1/2$. Given $>3$ ($\{4,5,6\}$): evens are $\{4,6\}$ → $2/3$. Since $1/2\ne2/3$, they're not independent.

E2easy

Compute $P(D\mid +)$ for prevalence 1%, sensitivity 99%, specificity 95% (verify the ≈17%).

Show solution
prev,sens,spec=0.01,0.99,0.95
sens*prev/(sens*prev+(1-spec)*(1-prev))   # 0.1667
E3medium

Keep sensitivity/specificity fixed; raise prevalence to 10%. What does $P(D\mid +)$ become, and why the jump?

Show solution

$\frac{0.99\cdot0.10}{0.99\cdot0.10+0.05\cdot0.90}=\frac{0.099}{0.144}\approx0.69$. Higher base rate ⇒ true positives now outnumber false alarms ⇒ posterior jumps from 17% to ~69%. The prior drives the answer.

E4medium

Two independent coin flips. $P(\text{two heads})$? $P(\text{at least one head})$? Show the second via the complement.

Show solution

Two heads: $0.5\times0.5=0.25$. At least one head $=1-P(\text{no heads})=1-0.25=0.75$.

E5hard

Tiny Naive Bayes: classes A (prior 0.3) and B (0.7); a word has $P(\text{word}\mid A)=0.8$, $P(\text{word}\mid B)=0.1$. Given the word appears, which class, and with what posterior?

Show solution
prior=np.array([0.3,0.7]); like=np.array([0.8,0.1])
post=prior*like/ (prior*like).sum()
post   # [0.774 0.226] -> class A wins despite lower prior

Posterior ≈ [0.77, 0.23] → A. The strong likelihood (0.8 vs 0.1) overrides B's larger prior.

InterviewQuestions you should be able to answer

Q1 · State the sum and product rules.
Product: $P(A,B)=P(A\mid B)P(B)$. Sum (marginalization): $P(A)=\sum_b P(A,b)$. Together they generate all of probability.
Q2 · Write Bayes' theorem and name each term.
$P(H\mid E)=\frac{P(E\mid H)P(H)}{P(E)}$ — posterior = likelihood × prior ÷ evidence. Posterior ∝ likelihood × prior.
Q3 · Why can a 99%-accurate test give a positive that's probably wrong?
Base rate. If the condition is rare, false positives from the large healthy population outnumber true positives, so $P(\text{sick}\mid +)$ is low despite high test accuracy.
Q4 · What does Naive Bayes assume?
That features are conditionally independent given the class, so their likelihoods multiply. Often false but frequently effective, especially for text.
Q5 · How do priors relate to regularization?
A prior over parameters biases the estimate toward certain values; MAP estimation maximizes likelihood × prior, and common priors (Gaussian/Laplace) correspond to L2/L1 regularization.

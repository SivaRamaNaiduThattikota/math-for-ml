Sampling & the CLT

Why averages of almost anything become Gaussian — the Central Limit Theorem, standard error, and the √n law behind confidence and A/B tests.

~20 min readNumPy paired notebookPrereq: Expectation & variance

Why is the Gaussian everywhere? Session 13 asserted it; this session explains it. Take almost any distribution — skewed, spiky, lumpy — draw a sample, and average it. Do that over and over, and the averages pile up into a bell curve, no matter what the original looked like. That's the Central Limit Theorem, one of the most surprising and useful facts in all of mathematics — and the foundation for confidence intervals, A/B tests, the bootstrap, and why "more data" reliably helps.

This session is about sampling: how a statistic computed from a sample behaves, why the sample mean is so well-behaved, and the √n law that governs how fast estimates sharpen.

By the end you will be able to
  • Distinguish a population, a sample, and a sampling distribution.
  • State that the sample mean has $E=\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2/n$, giving standard error $\sigma/\sqrt{n}$.
  • State the Law of Large Numbers and the Central Limit Theorem.
  • Apply the √n law — quadrupling data only halves the error.
  • Connect this to bootstrapping, mini-batch noise, ensembling, and A/B testing.

Notation reference

Section 1Population, sample & sampling distribution 📖 MML §6.4

The population is everything you could measure (all users, all possible rolls); its true mean $\mu$ and std $\sigma$ are usually unknown. A sample is the $n$ values you actually observe, from which you compute a statistic like the sample mean $\bar{X}_n$. The crucial insight: a statistic is itself random — draw a new sample and you get a different value. Its distribution over all possible samples is the sampling distribution, and understanding it is what lets you say how much to trust an estimate.

Your Power BI / SQL anchor Run AVG(revenue) on today's data, then on a different day's — you get different numbers even if nothing "really" changed. That wobble is the sampling distribution. This session quantifies it, so you can tell a real shift from sampling noise (exactly what A/B testing needs).

Section 2The sample mean is beautifully behaved

Because expectation is linear and (for independent draws) variances add (Session 14), the sample mean of $n$ i.i.d. draws has:

$$ E[\bar{X}_n]=\mu, \qquad \operatorname{Var}(\bar{X}_n)=\frac{\sigma^2}{n}, \qquad \text{so } \operatorname{SE}=\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} $$

Two consequences. First, it's unbiased — on average it hits the true mean. Second, its spread shrinks as $n$ grows: the Law of Large Numbers says $\bar{X}_n\to\mu$ as $n\to\infty$. Average enough data and you home in on the truth. The standard deviation of the sample mean, $\sigma/\sqrt{n}$, gets its own name: the standard error.

Section 3The Central Limit Theorem

The LLN says where the mean lands; the CLT says what shape its distribution takes. And the answer is astonishing:

$$ \bar{X}_n \;\xrightarrow{\ n\to\infty\ }\; \mathcal{N}\!\left(\mu,\ \frac{\sigma^2}{n}\right) \qquad\text{whatever the shape of the original distribution} $$

Sum or average enough independent pieces and the result is Gaussian — even if each piece is uniform, exponential, or wildly bimodal. Pick a deliberately un-Gaussian source below, then raise the sample size and watch the distribution of the means collapse into a bell.

Section 4Standard error & the √n law

The standard error $\sigma/\sqrt{n}$ has a humbling implication: precision improves with the square root of the data, not linearly.

  • To halve your error, you need 4× the data.
  • To cut it 10×, you need 100× the data.

This is why the first thousand examples help enormously and the millionth barely moves the estimate — diminishing returns baked into the mathematics of averaging.

Going deeper — the exact statement and its conditions

Formally, for i.i.d. $X_1,\dots,X_n$ with finite mean $\mu$ and finite variance $\sigma^2$, the standardized sample mean converges in distribution to a standard normal:

$$ \frac{\bar{X}_n-\mu}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}}\ \xrightarrow{\ d\ }\ \mathcal{N}(0,1) $$

Two conditions matter: the draws are independent and identically distributed, and the variance is finite (heavy-tailed distributions like Cauchy have no finite variance and the CLT fails). A common rule of thumb is $n\gtrsim30$, but skewed sources need more (watch the demo's Exponential need a larger n than Uniform). The $\sigma^2/n$ comes straight from Session 14: variances of independent things add, so averaging $n$ of them divides the variance by $n$. Builds on MML §6.4.

Section 5In NumPy ✓ outputs verified

import numpy as np

# CLT: means of an exponential (very skewed) source look Gaussian
means = [np.random.exponential(1.0, size=30).mean() for _ in range(5000)]
print(round(np.mean(means),3), round(np.std(means),3))  # ~1.0, ~1/sqrt(30)=0.183

# standard error and the sqrt(n) law
data = np.random.exponential(1.0, size=1000)
se = data.std(ddof=1) / np.sqrt(len(data))
print(round(se, 4))                     # standard error of the mean

# bootstrap: estimate the SE by resampling (no formula needed)
boot = [np.random.choice(data, size=len(data), replace=True).mean() for _ in range(2000)]
print(round(np.std(boot), 4))            # ≈ the formula's SE above
1.001 0.182 0.0316 0.0315 # bootstrap SE matches the σ/√n formula
The bootstrap: SE without a formula When there's no clean formula for a statistic's standard error (a median, an AUC, an R²), bootstrap it: resample your data with replacement many times, recompute the statistic each time, and take the std of those values. It's the CLT/sampling idea turned into a universal, code-only tool — heavily used to put error bars on ML metrics.

Section 6Where it lives in real ML

  • Confidence in metrics. Your model's accuracy on a test set is a sample mean — it has a standard error. Report error bars (bootstrap) instead of a single number.
  • Mini-batch gradients are sample means of per-example gradients, so their noise scales as $\sigma/\sqrt{\text{batch}}$ — bigger batches = less gradient noise (Session 11).
  • Ensembling / bagging averages many models; since variances of independent errors add and then divide by count, the ensemble's variance drops — the CLT's variance reduction, monetized.
  • A/B testing (Session 16) rests entirely on the sampling distribution of a difference in means being approximately Gaussian.
The one-line takeaway Averages of independent things are Gaussian with mean $\mu$ and standard error $\sigma/\sqrt{n}$ — that's the CLT. It's why the normal distribution is everywhere, why more data helps (but only as $\sqrt{n}$), and why we can put honest error bars on estimates.
Computational & memory cost A Monte-Carlo estimate's error shrinks like $1/\sqrt n$: to halve your uncertainty you need 4× the samples. That square-root law sets the cost of every simulation, A/B test, and sampling-based estimate — precision is bought by the square of the data.

ReferenceEdge cases, rules & traps

Edge cases & gotchas

  • The CLT is about the mean/sum, not individual values — averaging Gaussianizes; the raw data stays whatever shape it was.
  • Independence & finite variance required — correlated samples or heavy tails (Cauchy) break it.
  • Skewed sources need larger $n$ — the $n\ge30$ rule is rough; check, don't assume.
  • Standard error ≠ standard deviation — SE = $\sigma/\sqrt{n}$ describes the estimate's wobble, not the data's spread.

Golden rules

  1. The sample mean has $E=\mu$, $\operatorname{Var}=\sigma^2/n$, SE $=\sigma/\sqrt{n}$.
  2. LLN: the sample mean converges to the true mean as $n\to\infty$.
  3. CLT: the sample mean/sum becomes Gaussian regardless of the source's shape.
  4. √n law: to halve the error, quadruple the data.
  5. No formula for a statistic's SE? Bootstrap it by resampling.
Trap 1 · "My data will become normal with more samples" No — the CLT normalizes the average, not the raw data. A skewed variable stays skewed; only its sample mean's distribution turns Gaussian.
Trap 2 · Confusing SD and SE Standard deviation describes spread of the data; standard error describes uncertainty of the estimate and shrinks with $n$. Reporting SD where SE is meant overstates uncertainty (and vice-versa).
Trap 3 · Assuming the CLT always applies It needs i.i.d. draws and finite variance. Correlated observations (time series), tiny $n$ on skewed data, or heavy tails invalidate it — and A/B tests built on it then mislead.

Quick self-check

You quadruple your sample size. What happens to the standard error of the mean?
The CLT says that with large n, which becomes approximately Gaussian?

PracticeExercises

Hints only — solve in the paired practice.ipynb.

E1easy

Draw 5,000 means of 30 uniform(0,1) samples. What are the mean and std of those means (compare to $1/2$ and $\frac{1}{\sqrt{12\cdot30}}$)?

Show solution
m=[np.random.rand(30).mean() for _ in range(5000)]
np.mean(m), np.std(m)   # ~0.5, ~0.0527 (= (1/√12)/√30)
E2easy

Show the √n law: compute SE for n=100 and n=400 from the same source; confirm the second is half.

Show solution

SE ∝ $1/\sqrt{n}$, and $\sqrt{400}/\sqrt{100}=2$, so the n=400 SE is half the n=100 SE.

E3medium

Take a very skewed source (exponential). Histogram single draws vs means of 50. Which is Gaussian-looking?

Show solution

Single draws stay skewed (long right tail); the means of 50 look like a symmetric bell — the CLT acting on the average, not the raw data.

E4medium

Bootstrap the standard error of the median of a dataset (no clean formula exists).

Show solution
d=np.random.exponential(1,500)
boot=[np.median(np.random.choice(d,500,replace=True)) for _ in range(2000)]
np.std(boot)   # bootstrap SE of the median
E5hard

Ensembling: average the predictions of $k$ independent noisy models (each error std 1). Show the ensemble error std drops as $1/\sqrt{k}$ for $k=1,4,16$.

Show solution
for k in (1,4,16):
    err=np.random.normal(0,1,(100000,k)).mean(1)
    print(k, round(err.std(),3))   # ~1, ~0.5, ~0.25

Averaging $k$ independent errors cuts the std by $\sqrt{k}$ — exactly why bagging/ensembles reduce variance.

InterviewQuestions you should be able to answer

Q1 · State the Central Limit Theorem.
For i.i.d. variables with finite mean μ and variance σ², the sample mean's distribution approaches $\mathcal{N}(\mu,\sigma^2/n)$ as n grows — regardless of the original distribution's shape.
Q2 · LLN vs CLT?
LLN: the sample mean converges to the true mean (where it lands). CLT: the sample mean's distribution becomes Gaussian with SE σ/√n (what shape the wobble takes).
Q3 · What is the standard error, and the √n law?
SE = σ/√n, the std dev of the sample mean. Because of the square root, halving the error needs 4× the data — diminishing returns.
Q4 · What is bootstrapping and when do you use it?
Resampling the data with replacement to build a sampling distribution empirically — used to estimate standard errors / confidence intervals for statistics with no clean formula (median, AUC, model metrics).
Q5 · How does the CLT relate to ensembling?
Averaging k independent models' errors reduces the variance by k (std by √k), since independent variances add then divide — the same math that makes the sample mean precise.

Averages are normal, even when data isn’t.

Reusable idea: The Central Limit Theorem is why the Gaussian is everywhere.

  • Sample means cluster around the true mean, spread ∝,1/√n.
  • CLT: sums/averages of many effects → approximately Normal.
